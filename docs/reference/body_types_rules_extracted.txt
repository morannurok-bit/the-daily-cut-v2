Body Type: Rectangle
TL;DR: Rectangle needs either trace precision, real
anchor, real sculpt, or intentional edge/proportion play.
What fails is fake shape, limp anchor, clingy column,
bulky box, or unintentional length.
1. Identification
How users self-identify in Q2: "My shoulders, waist, and hips are about the same width."
Confirming signals from Q3 (distribution):
Snug zones — diagnostic:
● Chest and/or waist/stomach
● Nowhere specific (lean rectangles)
Snug zones — noisy / not diagnostic:
● Arms, hips/thighs, seat (these can fire for many reasons unrelated to rectangle
classification)
Loose zones — diagnostic:
● Seat (rectangles commonly have flat seats and back-gaping pants is a near-universal
complaint)
● Arms, hips/thighs (lean rectangles often report these as loose)
● Chest (lean rectangles)
Loose zones — noisy:
● Waist/stomach (a "loose at waist" answer suggests slim rectangle, not a contradiction)

Confirming signals from Q4 (fit issues):
Fit issues — diagnostic:
● Feels tight in the chest (wider ribcage rectangle)
● Feels tight in the waist (the fact that the natural waist isn't smaller than chest/hips means
standard tailoring assumes a taper that isn't there)
● Feels too loose around hips/seat (the universal flat-seat complaint)
● "Everything usually sits how it should" — also possible for lean balanced rectangles
Length issues — diagnostic:
● Pants feel too short / tops feel too cropped → if tall (rectangles tend to have longer legs
and arms; standard cuts run short on tall rectangles)
● Pants feel too long / tops feel too long → if petite (same proportional reality, just at
smaller scale)
● "Everything usually sits where it should" — common for mid-height rectangles around
165–172 cm
Conflicting or ambiguous signals to watch for:
● Snug at seat: fuller-bottomed rectangle exists. Treat as a real geometric variation.
Rectangle styling rules still apply (waist creation, vertical line moves, ribcage ease) but
suppress the "seat shaping" default — she has volume there already.
● Snug at arms or hips/thighs: common in non-lean rectangles. Doesn't indicate a different
body type. Use it to flag bulk-aversion in those zones but don't reclassify.
● Loose at waist: indicates slim rectangle. Don't interpret as evidence of a defined waist
(which would suggest hourglass). The waist is loose because the body is narrow
throughout, not because there's a taper.
● Snug at chest with no other signals: could be wider ribcage rather than fuller bust. If the
user didn't flag chest exposure as covered and didn't pick anchor strategies emphasizing
bust, default to ribcage interpretation.
●
Distinctions from adjacent body types:
● Rectangle vs. hourglass: hourglass has a measurable taper (waist 25%+ smaller than
bust/hips). Rectangle does not. The Q3 + Q4 combination (loose at seat, tight at waist,
gaping waistbands) reliably distinguishes.
● Rectangle vs. inverted triangle: inverted triangles have shoulders distinctly broader than
hips. Rectangles have balanced shoulders and hips. If user says shoulders are
broadest, it's inverted triangle, not rectangle.
● Rectangle vs. apple: apples carry weight specifically at the midsection, with the waist
being the widest point. Rectangles are uniform — waist isn't widest, just not smaller. If
user picks "fullness around midsection" she's apple, not rectangle.

2. Geometric Truth
Core proportions:
● Balanced shoulder, bust, and hip widths, generally within 5% of each other
● Undefined waist: typically less than 25% smaller than the bust
● Straight silhouette through the ribcage and hips
● Flat or minimally curved seat
Where volume sits:
● Tendency toward fullness: waist, midsection, chest (when present), upper arms
(sometimes)
● Tendency toward flatness: seat, hip curve
● Typically lean: arms, legs (proportionally)
Length tendencies:
● Legs longer than torso (consistent across heights)
● Arms tend to run long
● Tall rectangles (170+): legs read as a clear visual asset; can carry longer outerwear,
longer skirts, longer dresses
● Mid rectangles (165–169): proportions usually work in standard sizing without significant
alteration
● Petite rectangles (under 160): leg-to-torso ratio is preserved (legs still proportionally
longer than torso) but at smaller scale; standard cuts run long
Common geometric variations within the type:
● Wider-ribcage rectangle: fuller through the chest panel and ribcage. Needs ease through
the ribcage. Open necklines become high-priority relief moves. Closed/high necklines
combined with fitted ribcage panels read boxy fast.
● Narrow-frame rectangle: lean throughout. More styling tolerance — can wear a wider
range of cuts because the body itself is small in volume. Also more risk of "swallowing"
in oversized cuts; structure matters more.
● Fuller-bottom rectangle: has volume at the seat without hip flare. Seat-shaping defaults
are suppressed (volume already exists). All other rectangle rules apply.
● Athletic rectangle: more shoulder development, can carry structured shoulders well;
tailored outerwear becomes a particular hero.

3. Visual Assets (default, before strategy modulation)
Natural assets (read well without intervention):
● Long, slim legs: the most reliable rectangle asset. Proportionally longer than torso across
heights. Slim because rectangles don't carry hip flare.
● Lean arms: proportionally narrow. Sleeveless and short-sleeve cuts work without arm
self-consciousness for most rectangles.
● Décolletage and collarbones: the chest area below the neck and above the bust often
reads cleanly because rectangles tend not to have heavy bust-forward weight.
● Wrist and ankle: lean extremities. Become useful in edge strategy (cuffed sleeves,
cropped pants that hit just above ankle, wrist-tie details).
Features that become assets when used intentionally:
● Shoulders: can carry structured tailoring beautifully. Tailored blazers, structured
outerwear, defined shoulder lines — rectangles often look better in these than other
body types.
● Waist (created): through anchor or sculpt, a waist can be constructed where none exists.
A well-cinched rectangle reads more dramatic-hourglass than a natural hourglass
because the contrast is engineered.
● Vertical line: the rectangle's natural line is uninterrupted from shoulder to hip. With the
right styling (open layers, single-color outfits, length-emphasizing cuts), this becomes
elegance rather than boxiness.
Override conditions:
● Legs not an asset: if user covers legs in exposure, refuses short lengths, refuses
skirts/dresses entirely, or marks both relaxed/oversized on bottom AND no preference
for short — assume she doesn't want leg focus. Suppress short-length and slit-forward
recommendations.
● Décolletage not an asset: if user covers chest/neckline in exposure or specifically marks
chest as snug. Suppress open-neckline-as-relief defaults; treat closed/modest necklines
as preferred.
● Shoulders not a feature: if user is wider-ribcage rectangle who finds tailored items feel
boxy (rare; most rectangles benefit from tailoring). Trust her stated preferences here
over the default.
4. Visual Risks (default, before strategy modulation)
Silhouettes that fight this body type:

● Tight column dresses (closed neckline + no waist + no slit + midi/long): the body reads
as a flat rectangle painted onto a flat fabric rectangle. No vertical break, no interest, no
shape. Failure mode: "looks like a brick."
● Dropped waist: shifts the visual waistline below the natural waist, elongating the torso
and shortening the leg — exactly opposite to what rectangles need. The body's only real
asset (legs) is visually shortened.
● Over-the-waist jacket (mid-thigh outerwear with no shape): covers the only place a
rectangle could create a waist. Hides the proportion break and reads as a long tunic over
pants.
● Bulky knits and sweatshirts: horizontal volume on a body without horizontal taper
amplifies the boxy reading. The bulk doesn't drape over curves; it just sits.
● Clingy fabrics on top: shows the wider ribcage in detail, eliminates the relief that drape
would provide.
● Tight skirts with no release point: pencil skirts without slits, midi sheaths in stiff fabric.
The body has no natural taper to support; the skirt creates a flat column.
● Tank tops with wide straps: extends the shoulder line horizontally, emphasizing rectangle
shoulder-to-hip parallel.
● Slouchy pants with no gripping point: baggy through the waist, no defined waistband,
falling somewhere indeterminate. Loses the only place a rectangle can mark proportion.
● Boxy + clingy combinations: the worst pairing. Boxy cut shows the rectangle's lack of
taper; clingy fabric shows the body's actual width within the cut.
Construction details that fail:
● String-tie or drawstring "waist" on dresses and tops: the string implies a waist that isn't
there. The body doesn't fill the implied taper, so the dress hangs limp at the cinching
point. Drawstring and self-tie waists fail more severely on midsection-prone rectangles
— the gathered fabric at the cinching point doesn't just fail to create a waist, it adds
visible width at the body's widest point. Filter rather than deprioritize.
● Sculpt items with poor construction: thin sculpt fabric without proper underlining,
structured seams without the rigidity to hold them, "tailored" items at price points that
can't fund real tailoring. Sculpt that can't deliver is worse than no sculpt — it hangs
heavy and disappoints.
● Faux-belt details (sewn-on belts, decorative belts that don't cinch): implies waist creation
without delivering it.
● Empire waist: cuts the bust away from the torso and lets the rest of the dress fall as a
column. For a rectangle, this means losing the only proportion lever (waist creation)
while still covering the body in fabric. Exception: empire combined with full A-line skirt
can work; empire with column skirt fails.
● Wide horizontal pockets at the hip (cargo, patch): add bulk where rectangles don't need
it and break the vertical line at the wrong height.
● Pleated front pants without proper tailoring: add bulk at the hip and waist where
rectangles want clean lines. Pleat-front pants only work when properly tailored to fall flat.
Necklines that fail:

● Square / Sabrina necklines: add squareness to an already-rectangular frame. Direct
mechanism conflict.
● High closed crew, especially in fitted or thin fabric, on wider-ribcage rectangles: closes
off the relief zone the rectangle most needs. Combines with thin/light fabric to fail harder.
● Mock necks and turtlenecks in fitted fabric: shortens the visual neck, removes the
vertical break, can read boxy when paired with rectangle silhouette. Looser turtlenecks in
substantial fabric (drape away from the throat) can work; fitted ones fail.
Sleeves that fail:
● Cap sleeves on rectangles with broader shoulders: extends the horizontal further.
Combined with fitted bodice, reads boxy.
● Drop-shoulder dolman sleeves with no structure: broadens already-square shoulder line
into a bigger horizontal mass. Fails the bulk rule.
● Short tight sleeves that emphasize the shoulder-arm transition: awkward break point,
particularly on athletic or wider-ribcage rectangles.
● Puffed and dramatic statement sleeves for trace-only users: strategy mismatch. The user
picked the most conservative strategy; volume sleeves are an edge/sculpt move.
Fabric behaviors that fail:
● Clingy fabrics on top: especially thin viscose, slinky polyester, thin jersey. Shows ribcage
and midsection. Even on lean rectangles, this is unflattering because it removes drape
relief.
● Heavy stiff fabric in shapes that don't suit: heavy crepe in a column dress, structured
wool in a sheath without breaks. The fabric's weight emphasizes the rectangular outline.
● Flimsy fabric in cuts that need structure: a "tailored" blazer in soft drape can't hold its
shape; a "cinched" waist in flimsy fabric collapses. Rectangle silhouettes that depend on
construction need fabric that supports the construction.
Length traps:
● For tall rectangles: standard pants run short, exposing ankle when not intended;
standard tops cut at the wrong place.
● For petite rectangles: standard pants pool at the foot; midi skirts hit at the widest part of
the calf and visually shorten further; long dresses overwhelm the frame.
● For mid-height rectangles: usually no significant length traps unless they're at the
boundary of either tall or petite.
Override conditions (when "risk" items work):
● Boxy items: work when worn open and short, layered over a fitted base. The open
layered configuration creates the vertical line that closed boxy items destroy. A boxy
short jacket over a fitted tank with wide-leg pants reads sharp; a boxy long sweater over
the same pants reads heavy.

● Low-rise: works when paired with a top short enough to extend the visible torso —
sophisticated rectangles use this to balance leg length against torso length. If user
marked flexible on rise and didn't reject low-rise, allow it; pair recommendations with
shorter tops.
● Tight skirts: work when they have a release point — a back slit, a side slit, a kick pleat.
The release breaks the column.
● Heavy fabric: works in structured outerwear where the weight contributes to the structure
(a proper wool coat, a substantial blazer). Fails in unstructured shapes where the weight
just sits.
5. Strategy Modulation
Trace
What trace means for this body type: The most difficult strategy for rectangles. Trace asks
clothes to honor the body's natural shape — but rectangles don't have a shape that benefits
from being honored without modification. Trace on a rectangle works only when the items
selected have just enough construction to suggest shape while still tracking the body.
What works under trace:
● Controlled wide-leg trousers with a clear gripping point at the waist
● Fitted polo shirts (the rib structure provides gentle sculpt under trace)
● Fitted button-downs with a small flare at the hem
● Tailored blazers and coats (trace through the construction, not the body)
● Easy knits (not bulky, not clingy)
● Fitted jeans (mid or high rise; the rise creates the proportion the body lacks)
● Racer tops (vertical neckline, breaks the rectangle horizontally only at the strap)
● Halter tops (creates a V-line that breaks the parallel)
● Shift dresses
● Pencil skirts that release with a slit
● T-shirt dresses with a slit
● Three-quarter and cuffed sleeve cuts (clean, structured, no statement volume)
What fails under trace:
● Dresses tight from top to bottom without a break
● Dresses that read as one flat column (closed neckline + no waist + no slit + midi/long)
● Over-the-waist jackets
● Clingy fabrics on top
● Tight skirts with no release point
● Tank tops with wide straps

● Slouchy pants with no waist gripping point
● Boxy + clingy top combinations
● Statement sleeves and dramatic volume play
Special considerations: A rectangle who picks ONLY trace (no anchor, sculpt, or edge) has
effectively asked for the most restrictive strategy on her body type. Recommendations should be
few and precise. Don't widen the catalog to fill the page — better to show six excellent items
than thirty mediocre ones. Hero items get higher visibility; non-hero items still in catalog but
de-emphasized in proactive feed. This user is also more likely to be uncertain rather than
committed; consider whether her exposure to other styles might widen her range over time, and
treat behavior data as particularly valuable for refining her profile.
Anchor
What anchor means for this body type: Anchor creates the waist the body doesn't have.
Strong strategy for rectangles when executed correctly. Weakest when the anchor is fake
(drawstring on a column, sewn belt without cinching, wrap that doesn't actually cinch).
Hard vs. soft anchor: Rectangles need hard anchor. The waist must be created through real
garment construction or real cinching, not implied. Soft anchor (drapey wrap, drawstring tie,
loose belt) fails because there's no body shape underneath to fill out the implied waist. The
garment hangs limp at the cinching point and reads as ill-fitting.
Special exception: a substantial belt (wide leather, structured fabric belt with proper buckle) over
a relaxed dress can deliver hard anchor through the belt itself, even if the underlying garment is
soft. The belt has to be doing real work — visible cinching, structural buckle, enough width to
create a defined waistline. Thin self-tie strings don't qualify.
Specific anchor moves that work:
● A-line skirts, dresses, and coats
● Skater dresses (defined waist, full skirt)
● Tailored pants that cinch at the waist and release at the leg
● Flared tops (with caution for petite rectangles, who can be overwhelmed)
● Belted shirt dresses (with substantial belt, not self-tie)
● Tailored blazers with defined waist seaming
● Peplum tops and jackets
● Wide statement belts over relaxed tops
Anchor moves that fail:
● Self-tie waists, drawstrings, thin string belts on otherwise straight garments
● Drapey wrap dresses (the wrap doesn't cinch hard enough on a flat-waisted body)
● Flimsy fabric flares (the flare collapses without fabric support)

● Empire-line "waists" sitting under the bust (functions as a high column above and a
column below, unless paired with a full A-line skirt)
Sculpt
What sculpt means for this body type: Sculpt creates artificial shape. The body lacks a
defined waist; the garment imposes one through structured construction, rigid fabric, and
tailored seaming. Advanced technique, popular with fashion-forward rectangles. The work is
outsourced from the body to the item.
What good sculpt looks like for this body:
● Construction with proper interfacing, lining, and structural seams
● Mid-to-heavy fabric that holds the shape without sagging
● Defined waist seaming, princess seams, or boning where appropriate
● Fit that's close at the waist but doesn't compress the rest of the body
● Quality construction visible at hems, seams, finishings
What bad sculpt looks like:
● Heavy, rigid fabrics with poor tailoring (the fabric is doing the work the construction
should do; reads stiff and uncomfortable)
● Flimsy fabric that can't hold shape (the silhouette collapses; the dress promises sculpt
and delivers shapeless)
● Sculpt items at fast-fashion price points (the construction can't fund the tailoring; lining is
thin, structure is implied through fabric weight rather than built in)
● Sculpt cuts on the wrong proportions (waist defined too high or too low for the body; bust
shaping that doesn't match the user's bust)
Sculpt-friendly items:
● Wide-leg jumpsuits with wide structural belts
● Flared waistcoats
● Cinched blazers with proper construction
● Structured fit-and-flare dresses in substantial fabric
● Sculpt midi dresses with defined waist seams and A-line skirts
Edge
What edge means for this body type: Edge is one of the strongest strategies for rectangles —
when the user doesn't want to deal with body shape at all, edge redirects attention to assets and
uses proportion play to control the eye. Rectangles have clear assets edge can work with (legs,
arms, décolletage, vertical line) and clear risks edge can deflect from (the boxy reading, the
wider ribcage, the lack of waist).

Featuring vs. balancing modes for this body type: Both modes work. Many rectangles
operate in both at different times.
● Featuring mode: draws eye to legs (slits, short hems, leg-revealing cuts), arms
(sleeveless, cuffed sleeves, draped sleeves), or décolletage (open necklines, V-necks,
halters). Single-target attention play.
● Balancing mode: uses volume distribution to create a ratio the body doesn't naturally
have. Volume on top + slim bottom, or slim top + volume on bottom. Layering with
proportion intent (boxy short jacket over fitted base, fitted tank under wide-leg pants).
Edge moves that work:
● Short shift dresses (legs as feature)
● Long, relaxed/oversized dresses with fluid fabric and strategic openings (chest, slit,
sleeve)
● Big T-shirt with biker shorts or short jeans (proportion play, leg feature)
● Sheer long skirts (leg suggestion through fabric)
● Tunic dresses with multiple edge moves (slit + open neckline + sleeve detail)
● Open-back tops or dresses (where exposure permits)
● Wide-leg trousers + fitted tank + boxy short jacket (full balancing-mode look)
● Halter tops (vertical V-break + arm/shoulder feature)
● Cropped wide-leg pants (ankle feature)
● Volume-adding sleeves on otherwise simple silhouettes (bishop, bell, tulip)
Edge moves that fail:
● Big dresses that hold their shape rigidly without moving with the body (the volume reads
as architectural rather than expressive)
● Shapeless sacks with no openings or detail (volume without intent — reads as hiding
rather than playing)
● Big T-shirts that create bulk instead of balance (no proportion counterweight, no fitted
base, no leg reveal)
● Edge moves that draw attention to risks rather than assets (cropped tops that emphasize
the wider ribcage, low V-necks that show clinging fabric across the chest)
6. Combinational Rules
Rules that fire when specific multi-answer combinations occur, not derivable from any single
answer.
Body + strategy combinations:

● Rectangle + trace only: show few, precise items; hero-weighted surfacing; non-hero
items de-emphasized in feed
● Rectangle + anchor: hard anchor only; soft anchor items filtered out or flagged
● Rectangle + sculpt: require quality construction tags on sculpt items; filter out
low-construction sculpt at the catalog level
● Rectangle + edge: both featuring and balancing modes available; weight visual asset
moves and proportion play moves
● Rectangle + multiple strategies: the user is signaling sophistication. Widen the catalog
within rectangle constraints. Allow non-traditional moves (low-rise paired with short tops,
etc.)
Body + fit combinations:
● Rectangle + relaxed everywhere: shape must come from garment construction, not from
fit. Filter to relaxed items with intentional cut (structured shoulders, defined hems,
deliberate proportions). Filter out shapeless slouchy items.
● Rectangle + close everywhere: requires items with vertical line moves (open necklines,
V-necks, fitted with subtle flare). Avoid clingy fabrics; require fabric with body.
● Rectangle + relaxed top + close bottom: edge-balancing setup; lean into items that work
with this proportion (boxy short jacket + slim or fitted pant; relaxed tunic + leggings)
● Rectangle + close top + relaxed bottom: classic rectangle proportion play; lean into
wide-leg + fitted top combinations, halter or polo + wide-leg, tank + wide-leg + structured
short jacket
● Rectangle + oversized everywhere: highest-skill execution. Items must have intentional
structure and proportion. Filter out anything shapeless. Require visible construction
quality (substantial fabric, defined shoulders, deliberate hems).
Body + fabric combinations:
● Rectangle + cling-averse: filter out thin jersey, thin viscose, slinky polyester, anything
that traces ribcage or midsection. Require fabric with body.
● Rectangle + heavy-averse: filter out heavy wool coats, dense knits, thick canvas. The
body type doesn't carry weight well — bulk becomes boxy fast. Look for
structured-but-light: tropical wools, structured cottons, fine merinos, ponte with body.
● Rectangle + fabric importance high + sculpt: protection layer fires hard. Sculpt items
must have visible construction quality, real lining, substantial fabric. Filter out fast-fashion
sculpt regardless of how well it tags on shape.
Body + exposure combinations:
● Rectangle + edge + legs uncovered: leg-forward silhouettes weighted high. Short
dresses, slits, leg-revealing cuts.
● Rectangle + arms uncovered: sleeveless and short-sleeve cuts available; halter and
racer tops weighted high (arm + shoulder play)

● Rectangle + chest open: open necklines weighted high (V, scoop, halter, square, polo
open). This is also the default rectangle relief move regardless of strategy when chest
isn't covered.
● Rectangle + back uncovered + edge: open-back tops and dresses become hero items;
the back is a non-front asset surface for rectangles.
● Rectangle + midriff covered: filter out cropped tops, tops that ride up, thin fabrics that
show midsection. This is critical because rectangles often feel midsection
self-consciousness even when proportionally balanced.
Body + bust variation:
● Rectangle + chest snug + smaller frame signals: likely wider ribcage, not fuller bust.
Filter out fitted-bodice items. Prioritize ease through the chest panel. Weight open
necklines heavily. Bust-emphasis necklines (sweetheart, deep V, ruching at bust)
become risky rather than helpful.
● Rectangle + chest snug + fuller-bust signals: different inference. Weight V-necks at
medium depth, scoop necks. Filter out bust-emphasizing details. Surface tops with bust
accommodation that still create waist below the bust (princess seams,
empire-with-A-line).
● Rectangle + no chest snug: bust is neutral. Open necklines still default but no specific
bust-handling rules fire.
Body + height variation:
● Rectangle + petite + relaxed/oversized fit preference: highest-risk combination. Petite
rectangles in oversized cuts disappear most easily.
○ Required: structured oversized items only; reject shapeless oversized
○ No long outerwear (knee or below); favor mid-thigh max
○ Short outerwear ending at natural waist preferred
○ Cropped layering pieces only
○ Fabric must have body to hold proportions on a smaller frame
● Rectangle + petite + edge: edge moves must be smaller in scale. Small slit not big slit.
Modest open neckline not deep. Wrist exposure not full forearm. Petite rectangles can
absolutely do edge, but the moves are calibrated down.
● Rectangle + tall + relaxed/oversized: safer combination. Tall rectangles carry more
fabric. Allow longer outerwear, fuller silhouettes, more dramatic volume play.
7. Hero Categories
Hero items (consistent across rectangle):
● Controlled wide-leg trousers with clear gripping point (creates a waist; wide leg amplifies
bottom volume to balance with top)

● Fitted ribbed polo shirts (opens the chest, gentle ribcage sculpt, vertical line through
buttons)
● Button-downs (open necklines, vertical button line, can be worn open or closed for
register flexibility)
● Tailored blazers and coats (rectangles wear tailoring beautifully; structured shoulders
carry well)
● Easy knits — fine-gauge, structured, not bulky, not clingy
● Fitted jeans, mid-to-high rise (creates proportion the body lacks)
● Racer tops (breaks the rectangle vertically through neckline)
● Halter tops (V-line + arm feature)
● Shift dresses (clean line, often with vertical interest through detail)
● Pencil skirts that release with a slit
● T-shirt dresses with slit
● Wide-leg pants paired with fitted top + boxy short jacket (the layered balancing uniform)
Hero necklines:
● V-neck (shallow to medium depth): vertical break, elongates torso, draws eye to
décolletage
● Scoop neck: softens the angular frame, opens the chest panel
● Sweetheart: creates curves at the bust line where the rectangle is straight; calibrate
depth for fuller-bust users
● Halter: structurally tapered V-line + arm/shoulder feature
● Asymmetric necklines (symmetric and one-shoulder): diagonal break disrupts the
rectangle parallel
● One-shoulder / asymmetric off-shoulder: breaks the rectangle parallel through diagonal
line and removes one shoulder from the horizontal axis. Strongest break of any neckline
category. Calibrated by edge intensity for boldness.
● Polo open / Henley / button-placket openings: vertical break + button line + collar interest
● Boat neck and bateau: cautious — works for rectangles who want to balance hips by
adding shoulder breadth; fails for rectangles wanting vertical emphasis or who already
have broader shoulders
Hero sleeves:
For trace and conservative users:
● Three-quarter sleeves
● Cuffed straight sleeves
● Rolled sleeves on button-downs
● Clean long sleeves with cuff detail
● Sleeveless and tank cuts (lean arms are an asset)
For edge or sculpt users open to volume:

● Bishop sleeves (full from shoulder, gathered at wrist)
● Bell sleeves (fitted at shoulder, flaring at wrist)
● Tulip and flounce sleeves
● (Statement sleeves like dramatic puff or leg-of-mutton remain a style-zone preference,
not a body-zone default)
Hero items by strategy:
● Trace hero: fitted polo + fitted jeans, button-down + tailored trousers, shift dress
● Anchor hero: belted shirt dress with substantial belt, A-line midi dress, tailored blazer
with defined waist over wide-leg pant
● Sculpt hero: structured fit-and-flare dress, wide-leg jumpsuit with wide belt, cinched
waistcoat, tailored sheath with princess seams
● Edge hero: short shift dress, long tunic with slit and open neckline, halter dress, wide-leg
+ fitted tank + boxy short jacket combination
Hero outerwear:
● Tailored blazers (fitted at the waist or with subtle waist seaming) — strategy: trace,
anchor, sculpt
● Structured short jackets ending at the natural waist — strategy: edge, anchor (with fitted
base)
● Bombers and structured short jackets with cinched hem — strategy: edge, anchor
● Knee-length wool coats (only for tall rectangles or mid with belt) — strategy: anchor
(belted), trace
● Trench coats and structured belted coats — strategy: anchor
● Cropped leather or denim jackets ending at the waist — strategy: edge, anchor
● Jumpsuits with belted waist — universal hero, qualifier: belt must be substantial
Hero pant silhouettes:
● Wide-leg high-rise trousers, full length (tall) or ankle (mid/petite)
● Wide-leg high-rise jeans
● Straight-leg high-rise jeans (less amplification than wide-leg but cleaner line)
● Tailored cigarette-cut pants in fluid fabric (only for rectangles who chose close fit; pairs
with relaxed top)
● Bootcut high-rise (subtle flare creates ankle interest, balances slim hip)
Hero dress and skirt silhouettes:
● A-line dresses, knee-length to midi (height-dependent)
● Shift dresses, short to knee-length
● Skater dresses (defined waist, full skirt)
● Sheath with slit (only with release point)
● Wrap-style dresses with substantial cinching (not flimsy wraps)
● Tunic dresses with edge details (for edge users)

● Statement-sleeve dresses with column or A-line skirts (for users open to sleeve volume)
● A-line midi skirts
● Pencil skirts with kick pleat or slit
● Pleated midi skirts in structured fabric
Hero top silhouettes:
● Polo knits, especially with collar and 3-button placket
● Fitted button-downs with subtle hem flare
● Tank tops with narrow straps (not wide)
● Halter tops
● Racer-back tops
● Boxy short jackets and overshirts (for layering, not as standalone tops)
● Fitted ribbed knit tops
● Open-collar shirts in fluid fabric
Layering items (style-zone, not body-zone):
These are styling-dependent and should be surfaced based on user signals (edge or anchor
selection, sophistication signals) rather than as universal rectangle defaults:
● Vests, especially longer waistcoats with subtle waist taper
● Open cardigans (long for vertical line; cropped at waist for horizontal break)
● Open dusters and kimonos (vertical line, fluid layer)
● Cropped jackets ending at the natural waist
● Blazers worn open with belt over
Override conditions: petite rectangles get long dusters and kimonos filtered out (risk of
swallowing the frame). Users who didn't pick edge or anchor get layering items deprioritized.
8. Disqualified Categories
Items that don't work, regardless of strategy:
● Drop-waist dresses (shifts the visual waistline below natural; shortens the leg asset)
● Empire-waist dresses with column skirt (loses the only proportion lever)
● Tight straight column dresses with no neckline opening or slit
● Tube dresses, strapless column dresses without structural shaping
● Bulky chunky knit sweaters (especially crewneck, especially long)
● Boxy long shirts and tunics (closed and long; fights every rectangle rule)
● Wide-strap tank tops
● Slouchy pants without waist definition
● Fast-fashion sculpt items (heavy fabric, no real construction)

● Self-tie wrap dresses in flimsy fabric
● Drawstring-waist dresses on otherwise column silhouette
Items disqualified by specific answer combinations:
● Cropped tops disqualified for: rectangle + midriff covered
● Short skirts and dresses disqualified for: rectangle + legs covered
● Bodycon disqualified for: rectangle + relaxed/oversized fit preference
● Sleeveless disqualified for: rectangle + arms covered
● Backless or open-back disqualified for: rectangle + back covered
● Low rise disqualified for: rectangle + high or mid rise marked as preferred (only)
● Statement sleeves disqualified for: rectangle + trace-only strategy
Items that look right but disappoint:
● Wrap dresses: universally recommended in mainstream guides; on rectangles, the wrap
rarely cinches hard enough. Surface only wraps with substantial fabric and real cinching,
not soft self-tie wraps. (Default: filter out unless quality flags fire. This is a known
divergence from mainstream advice and is intentional.)
● Empire-line "fit-and-flare" dresses: photograph well but the empire seam loses the
rectangle's proportion lever unless the skirt is genuinely full A-line.
● "Tailored" items at fast-fashion prices: photograph as structured but lack the construction
to deliver. Protection layer fires especially hard for rectangle + fabric importance high.
● Ribbed knit dresses (long, midi): look polished in product shots, but cling to ribcage and
midsection while leaving no waist break. Fail mode for rectangles even when shape
suggests it should work.
● Pencil skirts in soft jersey: the cut implies structure; the fabric collapses. Looks fine on a
model but reads cheap and clingy on the body.
● "Shirt dresses" with thin self-tie belts: fake anchor. The thin tie can't cinch enough to
create real waist on a rectangle.
● Bare-tucked tops without further styling support: tucking a fitted top into pants without (a)
substantial belt over, (b) high-rise pants creating clear waist line, or (c) layering piece
adding shape can emphasize the lack of waist. Tucking is a real rectangle move but
requires support.
● T-shirt dresses without slit, belt, or layered structure on top: column shape on a column
body.
9. The Uniform
Daytime / casual uniform (mid-height, mid-sophistication, mixed strategy):
A pair of high-rise wide-leg trousers in a substantial cotton, soft wool, or structured linen blend,
in a neutral solid (black, navy, brown, ecru). Full length for tall, ankle length for mid/petite. The

trousers have a clear waistband — zip front or substantial elastic — that grips the natural waist
and releases into the wide leg. Fabric has body without being heavy.
On top, a fitted ribbed knit polo or a fitted tank tucked in. The top is fine-gauge, has structure
without bulk, and ends at the high hip or tucks cleanly. Open neckline is doing relief work for the
ribcage. If layering, a boxy short jacket — denim, suede, fine wool, or substantial cotton —
cropped to end at or just above the natural waist, with structured shoulders and intentional cut.
The jacket is worn open or buttoned depending on register; either way, the shoulder and waist
proportions hold.
If the outfit needs more anchor, a substantial belt at the natural waist — wide leather, statement
buckle, or a structured fabric belt that does real cinching. Not a thin self-tie or decorative
hardware.
Footwear: low-profile and clean. Pointed flats, simple ankle boots, loafers, minimal sneakers,
low heels for register. Nothing chunky.
Minimal jewelry, with permission for one statement piece if user signals interest.
Variations by strategy:
● Trace-primary rectangle: swap the boxy jacket for a tailored blazer that traces the body's
line gently. Polo shirt + tailored trousers. Less proportion play, more body honoring
through fitted construction.
● Anchor-primary rectangle: lead with the belt or with garment-internal anchor (belted
dress, peplum jacket). The waist is the visible focal point of the outfit.
● Sculpt-primary rectangle: swap separates for a structured dress (fit-and-flare with quality
construction) or a tailored jumpsuit with statement belt. The whole outfit is one sculpt
move.
● Edge-primary rectangle: lead with proportion play (wide-leg + tank + boxy short jacket) or
with a single-piece edge dress (tunic with slit, halter dress, short shift). Clothes are doing
attention play, not shape creation.
Variations by register:
● Work: swap denim for tailored wool or substantial cotton trousers; swap polo or tank for
a button-down or fine knit; structured blazer over.
● Evening: sculpt becomes more accessible. A fit-and-flare cocktail dress, a tailored
jumpsuit with belt, an open-back column dress with sculpted bodice. Fabric becomes
more luxe (silk, structured wool, satin-back crepe). Edge moves can be bolder (deeper
slit, lower V).
● Occasion: depends on event. Weddings and formal: structured midi dresses, tailored
sets, sculpt pieces. Cocktail: fit-and-flare or sculpt mini.
Variations by season:

● Summer: wide-leg cotton trousers or tailored shorts (only if legs are an asset); fitted
tanks, halter tops, polo shirts, boxy short button-downs worn open. Cotton, linen blends,
structured cotton — fabrics with body that don't cling. Sandals or simple flats. T-shirt
dresses with slit, short shift dresses, halter dresses for full-outfit options.
● Winter: substantial wool trousers, structured denim, layered knits with fine gauge.
Tailored coats — knee length for tall, mid-thigh for mid/petite. Substantial blazers under
outerwear. Layering with intentional proportion (fine knit tucked into trousers + cropped
jacket + structured coat). Avoid bulk in any single piece; layer thin pieces with structure.
10. Inference Notes for the Recommender
Default rules that fire on rectangle identification alone:
1. Legs are an asset by default; short lengths and slits are available unless explicitly
suppressed.
2. Vertical line moves are preferred (open necklines, button plackets, layered open jackets,
single-color outfits, length-emphasizing cuts).
3. Bulk is a risk; chunky knits, heavy unstructured layers, and horizontal-emphasis items
are filtered or downranked.
4. Cling at the top is a risk; thin/slinky/clingy fabrics are filtered or downranked for tops and
dresses.
5. Open necklines are a default relief move; closed/high necklines are downweighted
unless paired with other vertical moves.
6. Hard anchor preferred over soft anchor; filter out items where the anchor is implied
rather than constructed.
7. Sculpt items must have quality construction; protection layer fires for fabric content +
lining + structure.
8. Boxy items are downranked unless they're short and meant to be worn open.
9. Tailored shoulders work; surface tailoring options across categories.
10. Standard pant lengths run wrong for rectangles at the height extremes; surface tall and
petite cuts where available, and flag length-adjustability.
11. Necklines weighted toward open V, scoop, halter, polo, asymmetric, and one-shoulder.
Square and Sabrina filtered. Mock and turtleneck cautious.
12. Sleeves vary by strategy: trace gets clean sleeves and three-quarter; edge/sculpt
unlocks bishop, bell, tulip; dramatic statement sleeves remain a style-zone preference.
13. Layering items are styling-zone, not body-zone. Surface for users who picked edge or
anchor or who signaled sophistication. Suppress for petite users without explicit interest.
Cropped-at-waist preferred over cropped-above-waist.
14. Bust signals (chest snug + frame size hints) determine ribcage-vs-bust interpretation.
Different downstream filters fire for each.
15. Petite + oversized fires the highest-restriction rule set: structured items only, short
outerwear only, fabric with body required.

Modulators that change defaults:
● Strategy selection modulates default emphasis: trace → conservative item set with
hero-weighted surfacing; anchor → prioritize hard-anchor items; sculpt → require quality
construction tags; edge → unlock both featuring and balancing modes.
● Fit preferences modulate default fits: relaxed everywhere → require structure in items;
close everywhere → require body-having fabric, not clinging; mixed top/bottom →
emphasize proportion play items.
● Fabric importance modulates protection layer: high importance + sculpt → strict
construction filter; high importance + relaxed fit → strict drape and fabric body filter.
● Exposure modulates asset access: legs covered suppresses leg-forward defaults; chest
covered suppresses open-neckline defaults; midriff covered adds cropped/thin/short top
filter; back covered removes open-back items.
● Avoidances override everything: hard avoidances filter at floor level regardless of how
well items match elsewhere.
Sophistication and restrictiveness signals:
Sophistication signals (widen the catalog):
● Multi-strategy selection (3+)
● Multiple "I'm flexible" answers across rise, length, cut
● Broad exposure tolerance
● Varied fit preferences (e.g., close top + relaxed bottom)
These signals widen the recommendation feed: larger volume, mixed hero-and-variety, more
discovery surface. The user is trusted to filter at her own taste level. Allow non-traditional
moves: low-rise paired with short tops, intentional volume play, items that require styling
intelligence.
Restrictiveness signals (tighten the catalog):
● Trace-only or single-strategy selection
● Multiple covered exposure zones
● Strict fabric requirements ("very important" + multiple discomfort flags)
● Petite identification
● Specific avoidances across rise, length, and cut
These signals tighten the recommendation feed: smaller volume, hero-weighted ranking,
conservative variety. Non-hero items remain in the catalog but receive less visibility in the
proactive feed. Better to show ten well-chosen items than fifty mixed ones for a closed user.
Risk-scanning patterns common to rectangles:
● How a top sits across the chest and ribcage (does fabric pull or pucker)
● Whether a dress has any waist break or reads as a column

● Whether an item's "structure" comes from construction or from fabric stiffness
● How a knit hangs — drape vs. cling vs. bulk
● Whether a "belt" or "waist tie" looks like real cinching or decoration
● Pant length on the model (does it pool, does it hit cleanly)
● Sleeve and shoulder placement (especially for tailored items)
Common "knows the trick" non-traditional moves:
● Sophisticated rectangles may use low-rise to balance long legs against shorter torso.
Don't filter low-rise out by default for rectangles who marked flexible on rise.
● Sophisticated rectangles may wear bodycon with confidence when fabric quality is high
and construction holds. Don't filter bodycon out by default; surface only quality-tagged
versions.
● Sophisticated rectangles may wear oversized intentionally (true edge balance work).
Don't restrict to fitted defaults if user picked oversized; surface structured-oversized
options.
● Sophisticated rectangles may use horizontal stripes or wide details intentionally for
proportion play (counter to conventional advice). Allow these when other signals indicate
sophistication.
On rule restrictiveness vs. user variation:
The rules optimize for the population of users in this profile, not for every individual variation
within it. Some rectangles will wear items the rules filter out and look great in them —
bust-forward rectangles in column dresses, very lean rectangles in volume play, rectangles with
confidence in midriff exposure. The rules correctly do not surface these items proactively,
because the cost of surfacing them to the median rectangle is higher than the cost of missing
them for the variation user. Variation users can still find items through search, browse, and
behavior data over time. The system's job is reliable filtering, not exhaustive personalization.
11. Open Questions and Edge Cases
Cases the rules don't yet handle:
● Rectangle + apple borderline: user with rectangle proportions but specific midsection
self-consciousness. How much of apple's "extend the visual midsection" rules apply?
Probably some — surface vertical-line moves more aggressively, suppress horizontal
interest at the waist.
● Rectangle + significant height extremes (very tall over 178, very petite under 152):
standard pant and dress length recommendations need explicit alteration tagging. May
need separate sub-profiles.
● Rectangle who picked sculpt but rejected hard fabric, structured construction signals:
contradictory profile. Likely user wants the idea of sculpt without the execution rigor;

could either filter sculpt items entirely or show only the highest-quality sculpt items with
caveat.
● Rectangle with full bust who didn't flag chest as snug: bust may be a feature she's
neutral on. Default open-neckline rules should still fire but with awareness that V-necks
may need to be moderate, not deep.
Borderline body-type identifications:
● Rectangle vs. mild hourglass: user with a slight waist taper (15–20% smaller than
bust/hips) who picked hourglass language but shows rectangle fit issues (gaping
waistbands, flat seat). Treat as rectangle for styling purposes; the geometric reality
matters more than the self-identification.
● Rectangle vs. inverted triangle: user with broader shoulders relative to hips but balanced
enough that she picked rectangle. If shoulder broadness is mild, rectangle rules work. If
shoulders are clearly broader, switch to inverted triangle defaults.
● Lean rectangle vs. straight athletic: essentially the same body type for styling purposes;
treat as rectangle with high confidence on tailoring and structured pieces.
● Petite rectangle vs. petite hourglass: most differentiating signal is fit issue patterns.
Petite hourglass complains about waist gap; petite rectangle complains about
column-shaped clothing burying her.
Things behavior data should resolve:
● Color and wash preferences (within neutral solids, prints, or specific palettes)
● Specific brand or fit affinities (some brands cut better for rectangles than others)
● Tolerance for various edge intensities (small slit vs. large slit, modest open neckline vs.
deep V)
● Register split sharpness (how different is her evening profile from her day profile)
● Seasonal preferences and category interest (does she shop dresses, separates,
outerwear in different proportions)
● Specific hero pieces unique to her wardrobe formula that emerge from repeated
selections
● Statement detail tolerance (statement sleeves, decorative collars, embellishment) —
captured in style profile, not body profile
● Style register preferences (polished vs. casual vs. fashion-forward) — captured in style
profile

Body Type: Inverted
Triangle
1. Identification
How users self-identify in Q2: "My shoulders are the broadest part of my body."
Confirming signals from Q3 (distribution):
Snug zones — diagnostic:
● Shoulders and/or arms
● Chest
● Waist/stomach (tops cut for proportional bodies pull across the upper body)
Snug zones — noisy / not diagnostic:
● Hips/thighs, seat (these can fire for many reasons unrelated to inverted triangle)
Loose zones — diagnostic:
● Seat (inverted triangles commonly have flat seats and narrow hips)
● Hips/thighs (the bottom half is narrower than the top, so standard pants gap or sit loose)
● Arms (if lean or athletic-leaning)
Loose zones — noisy:
● Waist/stomach (a "loose at waist" answer suggests narrow-frame inverted triangle, not a
contradiction)
Confirming signals from Q4 (fit issues):
Fit issues — diagnostic:
● Feels tight in the chest (top half carries more volume)
● Feels tight in the shoulders or arms
● Tops don't button across the bust or shoulder
● Feels too loose around hips/seat (universal narrow-bottom complaint)
● Bottoms gap at the back waistband (hip narrowness means pants cut for proportional
hips don't fill out at the back)

● "Everything usually sits how it should" — possible for lean inverted triangles where
overall scale is small enough that proportion mismatch is muted
Length issues — diagnostic:
● Pants feel too short / tops feel too cropped → if tall
● Pants feel too long / tops feel too long → if petite
● "Everything usually sits where it should" — common for mid-height inverted triangles
around 165–172 cm
Conflicting or ambiguous signals to watch for:
● Snug at seat: possible for inverted triangles who carry more weight at the bottom despite
shoulder dominance. Treat as a real geometric variation. Inverted triangle rules still apply
(balance shoulders down, add bottom volume, avoid bust/shoulder emphasis) but the
"add visual hip volume" default softens — she has hip volume.
● Loose at waist: indicates narrow-frame inverted triangle, often lean throughout the body
with the shoulder breadth being the only meaningful proportion signal. Don't reclassify as
rectangle; the geometric reality is shoulders > hips even if she's slim.
● Snug at chest with no other signals: could be fuller bust without shoulder breadth (would
suggest hourglass with bust) or could be wider ribcage. If shoulders are explicitly
described as broadest, trust that signal and treat as inverted triangle.
● Snug at arms specifically: athletic inverted triangle pattern. Shoulders are developed,
arms are toned but proportional to shoulders. Surface this as a known variation.
Distinctions from adjacent body types:
● Inverted triangle vs. hourglass: hourglass has balanced shoulders and hips with a
defined waist (25%+ taper). Inverted triangle has broader shoulders than hips with no
defined waist. If shoulders > hips and waist isn't tapering sharply, it's inverted triangle.
● Inverted triangle vs. rectangle: rectangles have balanced shoulders and hips. Inverted
triangles have shoulders distinctly broader than hips. The diagnostic question is whether
shoulders are the widest point of the body — if yes, inverted triangle; if shoulders match
hips, rectangle.
● Inverted triangle vs. apple: apples have weight at the midsection with shoulders and hips
narrower than the middle. Inverted triangles have weight at the upper body
(shoulders/chest) with the midsection narrower than the shoulders. If the widest point is
the shoulders, it's inverted triangle; if the widest point is the stomach, it's apple.
● Inverted triangle vs. athletic build: substantial overlap. Athletic builds with developed
shoulders and slim hips are inverted triangles. The rules apply the same way.
2. Geometric Truth

Core proportions:
● Shoulders are the broadest point of the body, distinctly wider than hips
● Bust often fuller, contributing to upper-body dominance
● Ribcage may also be wider than the lower half
● Waist is undefined or straight — not a sharp taper
● Hips are narrow relative to shoulders
● Seat is typically flat or minimally curved
● Legs tend to be lean and proportionally longer than torso
Where volume sits:
● Tendency toward fullness: shoulders, upper back, bust, upper arms (sometimes)
● Tendency toward flatness: seat, hips
● Typically lean: legs, lower body generally
● Midsection: usually straight rather than full; can be lean or carry mild weight depending
on individual
Length tendencies:
● Legs proportionally longer than torso (the "swimmer's build" pattern)
● Some inverted triangles have shorter torso with longer legs; others have proportional
torso. The longer-leg version is more common.
● Tall inverted triangles (170+): legs read as a strong visual asset; the shoulder breadth is
balanced by overall body length
● Mid inverted triangles (165–169): proportional in standard sizing
● Petite inverted triangles (under 160): shoulder breadth more visually dominant on
smaller frame; harder to balance
Common geometric variations within the type:
● Athletic inverted triangle: visible muscle development in shoulders and arms, contributing
to upper-body dominance through muscle rather than weight. Often very lean. Carries
structured tailoring beautifully but cap sleeves and structured shoulders amplify what's
already there. Often pairs with edge or sculpt strategies.
● Bust-forward inverted triangle: shoulders broad and bust also full. Upper body
dominance comes from both width and depth. Open necklines and V-necks balance
differently than for athletic types — they break the chest visually but bust still draws
attention. Requires careful neckline calibration. May unlock bust-as-edge featuring move
if user opts in.
● Narrow-frame inverted triangle: lean throughout, with shoulders being the only
meaningfully wider point. Has the most styling tolerance of the three variations because
total body volume is small. Can carry items that would overwhelm broader inverted
triangles.

● Hip-fuller inverted triangle: shoulders are still broadest but the lower body isn't as narrow
as typical. Closer to rectangle territory but with shoulder dominance preserved. Inverted
triangle rules still apply but the "add hip volume" default softens.
3. Visual Assets (default, before strategy modulation)
Natural assets (read well without intervention):
● Long, slim legs: the most reliable inverted triangle asset. Bottom half is naturally lean
and proportionally longer than torso. Strong leg-forward potential.
● Narrow waist (relative to shoulders): even though the waist isn't defined by taper, it's
narrow compared to the shoulder line, which means waist-marking can create dramatic
visual narrowing.
● Décolletage and collarbones: often a feature for inverted triangles since the upper chest
area is prominent without being heavy.
● Strong shoulders (when desired): for users who like the strong-shouldered look, this is a
natural asset. For users who want to soften, it's a risk to balance.
● Lean lower body: hips, thighs, seat all tend to be slim. Bottom-fitting clothes work without
hip self-consciousness.
● Lean ankles: often paired with the long-leg pattern.
Features that become assets when used intentionally:
● Hips and seat (visually added through clothing): wider hems, A-line skirts, full pants can
create visual hip volume that balances the shoulders. The body doesn't have hip volume
naturally but the silhouette can borrow it.
● Waist (created through anchor): a defined waist breaks up the column from shoulder to
hip and creates the hourglass illusion. Anchor strategy is the most powerful tool for
inverted triangles.
● Length of leg (extended visually): high-rise bottoms + leg-revealing cuts can emphasize
the leg asset and pull attention down from the shoulders.
● Bust (when fuller and user opts to feature): sweetheart, deeper V, surplice necklines turn
the bust into a featured asset rather than a proportion problem. Requires user signal that
bust is for featuring, not minimizing.
Override conditions:
● Legs not an asset: if user covers legs in exposure, refuses short lengths, refuses
skirts/dresses entirely. Suppress short-length and slit-forward recommendations. (Legs
are such a strong default asset for inverted triangles that this suppression is rare; most
welcome leg-forward styling.)
● Décolletage/chest not an asset: if user covers chest/neckline in exposure or marks chest
as snug and wants to deemphasize. Default open-neckline strategy still applies (open

necklines break the chest panel) but bust-emphasis necklines (sweetheart, deep V)
should be suppressed.
● Shoulders should be minimized: if user signals dissatisfaction with shoulder width
(sometimes through avoidance of certain top types in behavioral data). Suppress any
defaults that emphasize shoulders; emphasize softening through fabric and cut.
● Bust not for featuring: default. Even with fuller bust, treat as a proportion-problem zone
unless user explicitly opts into bust-featuring through exposure preferences and
behavior.
4. Visual Risks (default, before strategy modulation)
Silhouettes that fight this body type:
● Boxy tops that emphasize shoulder width: drop-shoulder, boxy crew, oversized
sweatshirts that hang straight from the shoulder line. They amplify the broadest point.
● Closed high necklines on broad shoulders: combined with shoulder width, closed
necklines create a solid wall across the upper body.
● Detailed or embellished tops: anything that adds visual interest to the upper body (ruffles
at the shoulder, statement collars, bold prints on top, bright colors on top alone) draws
attention exactly where the body is already drawing attention.
● Puff sleeves, leg-of-mutton, dramatic shoulder volume: adds width to the already-wide
shoulder. Disqualified except in very narrow-frame inverted triangles who can absorb the
volume.
● Structured shoulder pads: same problem — amplifies the existing structure.
● Tight column dresses with no waist break: the lack of waist combined with shoulder
dominance creates a body that reads as triangular, not hourglass.
● Pencil skirts that are too tight at the hip: emphasize the narrow lower half by clinging to
it, increasing the visual contrast between top and bottom.
● Cropped tops that end at the rib (drawing eye to ribcage/bust): extends the visual upper
body further.
● Slim/skinny jeans: don't balance the shoulder-hip proportion mismatch and can
emphasize narrow lower body. Not a universal failure mode — some users prefer them
for featuring the lean lower half (similar to edge-featuring logic). Allow by default with low
priority; promote when user signals close-to-body bottom preference; filter only when
user explicitly rejects skinny.
Construction details that fail:
● Structured shoulder pads
● Padded bust details or push-up construction: adds upper-body volume
● Horizontal seaming across the chest or shoulders: breaks the body horizontally at the
widest point

● Embellishment at the bust or neckline: draws eye to the upper body
● Bows, ruffles, or details at the shoulder or chest: amplifies the upper-body focus
Fabric behaviors that fail:
● Stiff fabric on top: doesn't drape over the shoulders softly; the rigidity makes shoulders
read sharper and broader
● Heavy or thick fabric on top: adds visual bulk where the body is already dominant
● Clingy fabric on top with full bust: shows the bust outline and emphasizes upper-body
weight
● Shiny fabric on top alone: light reflection draws the eye
Necklines that fail (default):
● Boat neck: extends shoulder horizontally
● Wide square neck: squares the already-wide top
● Off-shoulder, bateau, Sabrina: present full shoulder breadth without strap interruption
● High closed crew on its own: creates wall across upper body
Length traps:
● For tall inverted triangles: similar to rectangle — standard tops run short and create
awkward crop at the rib
● For petite inverted triangles: shoulder breadth is harder to balance because lower-body
volume options are more constrained; midi skirts hitting at calf-widest are extra
problematic because they shorten the leg asset that's most needed for balance
● For mid-height: usually no length traps unless very specific cuts
Override conditions:
● Boxy tops: work when worn open over a fitted base (same logic as rectangle), but the
boxy outer layer must not have structured shoulders. A soft drape boxy shirt worn open
over a fitted tank can work; a structured boxy blazer fails.
● Detail on top: works when paired with strong visual interest on the bottom that competes
for attention. A printed top + plain bottom fails; a plain top + printed full skirt is the classic
inverted-triangle balancing move. Reversing the placement of visual interest is the key.
● Statement sleeves: almost universally fail for inverted triangle. Rare exceptions for very
narrow-frame users who want to play with proportion intentionally.
5. Strategy Modulation
Trace

What trace means for this body type: The riskiest strategy for inverted triangle. Trace honors
the body's natural shape — but the inverted triangle's natural shape is exactly the proportion
problem the user often wants to balance. Trace tops will trace the shoulders (emphasizing
them); trace bottoms will trace the narrow hips (emphasizing the contrast). A user picking
trace-only is signaling either (a) very narrow-frame where the proportion mismatch is muted, (b)
athletic body she's proud of and wants to feature, or (c) doesn't know what other strategies offer.
What works under trace:
● Open V-neck and scoop tops (skim the body but break the chest vertically)
● Button-downs worn open or partially open
● Mid-to-high rise straight or wide-leg pants (skim the lower body, the rise creates implied
waist)
● Fitted A-line or full skirts (skim the waist, flare at the hip to add lower-body volume)
● Wrap tops that follow the body but cinch the waist
● Soft drape blouses in fluid fabric
● Trousers with subtle hip detail (pleats, pockets) that add visual interest at the lower body
What fails under trace:
● Tight tops at the shoulder/chest
● Snug button-downs that pull at the bust
● Tight column dresses
● Snug pencil skirts at narrow hips
● Anything that hugs the upper body fully
Special considerations: A user picking trace-only signals limited strategy options.
Recommendations should be carefully selected toward items that follow the body without
emphasizing the proportion mismatch. Hero-weighted surfacing applies (similar to rectangle +
trace-only). Better to surface eight precise items than thirty mediocre ones.
Anchor
What anchor means for this body type: The strongest strategy for inverted triangle. Anchor
creates a defined waist that breaks the column from shoulder to hip and adds visual volume
below the waist, which balances the upper body. Anchor engages directly with the proportion
problem through silhouette construction — creating the shape the body lacks. The single
strongest move for shifting the body's visual weight downward.
Hard vs. soft anchor: Both work for inverted triangles, unlike for rectangles. The waist is
narrow enough that even soft anchor (drawstring, wrap-tie, gentle gathering) can pull in and
create visible definition. The body doesn't have a defined waist by taper, but it's narrow enough
to support implied waist treatment.

Special exception: belts at the waist work but must be calibrated. A wide belt at the natural waist
can shorten the torso visually on inverted triangles whose torso is already proportionally short.
Medium-width belt or a belt at a slightly lower position (high hip) can work better.
Specific anchor moves that work:
● A-line skirts (creates lower-body volume below a defined waist)
● A-line dresses
● Wrap dresses (cinches the narrow waist, often has bias drape on top that softens
shoulders)
● Skater dresses (fitted bodice + full skirt, very strong for inverted triangle)
● Fit-and-flare dresses
● Belted shirt dresses with full skirt below
● High-rise wide-leg or flare pants (the waist hits at natural waist, the flare adds lower-body
volume)
● Peplum tops (creates artificial hip volume at the natural waist line)
● Drawstring or wrap tops over fitted bottoms
Anchor moves that fail:
● Cropped peplum tops that hit at the rib (defeats the waist-marking goal)
● Belts at the empire line (under the bust — extends the upper body further)
● Drop-waist styles (drops the visual waist below natural, elongating the upper body and
shortening the leg)
● Belts so wide they shorten the torso on petite inverted triangles
Sculpt
What sculpt means for this body type: Sculpt creates artificial waist and lower-body shape.
Effective for inverted triangles when the sculpt construction adds visual hip volume or shapes a
defined waist. Sculpt that emphasizes the upper body fails — push-up bust, structured
shoulders, fitted bodices without a waist break.
What good sculpt looks like for this body:
● Fit-and-flare construction with structured bodice and full skirt — the bodice defines the
waist, the skirt adds hip volume
● Sculpt midi dresses with A-line or fishtail skirts
● Tailored peplum jackets that hit at the natural waist
● Princess-seamed dresses that bring the eye inward at the waist
● Corseted or boned bodice with skirt volume below
What bad sculpt looks like:
● Sculpt with structured shoulders or shoulder pads (amplifies the existing upper-body
dominance)

● Sculpt that fits closely all the way down with no skirt volume (creates a tight V-shape
rather than balanced shape)
● Sculpt in heavy fabric that doesn't drape at the shoulders
● Sculpt with detail or embellishment at the bust
Sculpt-friendly items:
● Fit-and-flare cocktail and midi dresses
● Tailored peplum blazers in soft (not stiff) fabric
● Bustier-style dresses with full skirts (only when chest exposure is comfortable)
● Sculpt jumpsuits with wide-leg trousers (the wide leg adds lower-body volume that
balances the sculpt's waist definition)
Edge
What edge means for this body type: Edge bypasses the body's proportion problem rather
than solving it through silhouette engineering. Attention is redirected to specific features or
zones so the body's overall shape isn't the dominant visual signal. Unlike anchor (which creates
the missing waist) or sculpt (which builds the missing shape), edge leaves the silhouette alone
and changes where the eye lands.
Featuring modes (single-target attention):
● Legs: short hems, slits, leg-revealing cuts. The most accessible default for inverted
triangle since legs are typically a natural asset.
● Bust: for users with fuller bust who have chest exposure permitted and have signaled
bust as an asset to feature, featuring bust through deeper V, sweetheart, or surplice
necklines. Counterintuitive but works when the bust is the asset the user wants to
feature rather than minimize. Requires explicit opt-in via combination signals (see
section 6).
● Décolletage: open neckline showing collarbones without significant bust emphasis.
Works for users with smaller bust who want to feature the upper chest area as elegant
rather than dominant.
● Open back: redirects attention to the back as alternate focal point, away from front-facing
shoulder breadth.
● Ankle: cropped pants showing the ankle, statement footwear. Lean ankles are common
for inverted triangle.
Attention-redirect modes (visual interest pulling eye away from upper body):
● Color blocking with dark on top + light/bright on bottom: the standard inverted triangle
styling move. Outfit-level logic, not item-level.
● Statement bottoms with plain tops: printed skirts, patterned pants, embellished bottoms
paired with simple tops. The eye lands on the bottom item, not the body's silhouette.

● Print or detail placement below the waist: the print itself is the attention-grabber, located
in the area that benefits from added visual weight.
Edge moves that fail:
● Statement tops with plain bottoms (places attention exactly where the body is already
drawing it)
● Bold prints, bright colors, or detail on top alone (without compensation on bottom)
● Embellishments at neckline, shoulders, or bust
● Light or bright colors on top with dark on bottom (reverses the desired attention flow)
Note on edge vs. anchor confusion: A-line skirts, fit-and-flare dresses, wrap dresses, peplum,
and other items that create hip volume or waist definition are anchor moves, not edge. They
engage with the proportion problem directly through construction. Edge is reserved for moves
that bypass the silhouette question — featuring a body part as the focal point, or using
color/print to redirect attention away from the upper body without changing the underlying
shape.
6. Combinational Rules
Rules that fire when specific multi-answer combinations occur, not derivable from any single
answer.
Body + strategy combinations:
● Inverted triangle + trace only: show few, precise items; hero-weighted surfacing;
non-hero items de-emphasized. Trace is the most restrictive strategy for this body type.
● Inverted triangle + anchor: surface anchor items aggressively; this is the strongest
strategy for the body type. Both hard and soft anchor accessible.
● Inverted triangle + sculpt: require sculpt that defines the waist AND adds lower-body
volume. Filter sculpt that emphasizes the upper body alone.
● Inverted triangle + edge: unlock featuring modes (legs, décolletage primarily; bust only
with explicit opt-in) and attention-redirect modes (color/print logic at the outfit level).
Balancing-via-anchor is not edge — that's anchor.
● Inverted triangle + multiple strategies: sophistication signal. Widen the catalog. Allow
non-traditional moves and items that require styling intelligence.
Body + fit combinations:
● Inverted triangle + relaxed top + close bottom: fights the body's natural proportions.
Relaxed top widens the already-wide upper body; close bottom emphasizes the narrow
lower body. Filter out unless the relaxed top is open and the close bottom has hip
volume detail.

● Inverted triangle + close top + relaxed bottom: classic inverted triangle balancing setup.
Surface aggressively. Wide-leg + fitted top, tank + full skirt.
● Inverted triangle + close everywhere: requires items with strong waist creation (anchor
through cut). Filter out fitted columns and snug fits at the upper body. Also signal that
skinny/slim bottoms are user-preferred — promote them despite the proportion
mismatch.
● Inverted triangle + relaxed everywhere: requires structure in the bottom to provide hip
volume. Filter shapeless oversized. Require A-line or full silhouettes on the bottom.
Body + fabric combinations:
● Inverted triangle + cling-averse: filter out thin jersey, thin viscose, slinky polyester on
tops especially. The upper body should drape softly, not show contour.
● Inverted triangle + heavy-averse: applies more to tops than bottoms. Heavy structured
fabric on top adds visual mass to the already-dominant upper body. Bottoms can be
more substantial (a heavy A-line skirt provides good hip volume).
● Inverted triangle + soft drape preferred on top + structured on bottom: the inverse of
rectangle. Fluid drape on top softens shoulders; structured bottom holds the volume that
balances the shoulders.
Body + exposure combinations:
● Inverted triangle + legs uncovered: leg-forward silhouettes weighted high. Short dresses,
slits, leg-revealing cuts. Critical for balancing the upper body.
● Inverted triangle + chest covered: significant constraint. Most softening necklines (V,
scoop) become unavailable, leaving fewer options to break the chest panel. Surface
high-but-detailed necklines (mandarin, henley, button-up) instead.
● Inverted triangle + back uncovered + edge: open-back items become hero. Redirects
attention from the front-facing shoulder breadth.
● Inverted triangle + shoulders covered: prefers sleeves and shoulder coverage. Limits
asymmetric and one-shoulder options.
Body + bust variation combinations:
● Inverted triangle + fuller bust + chest exposure comfortable + bust signaled as asset:
unlock bust-as-edge featuring. Sweetheart, deeper V, surplice tops become accessible.
This is parallel to the bust-forward unlock for rectangle plunging styles, with similar opt-in
conditions.
● Inverted triangle + fuller bust + chest covered or bust to be minimized: maintain default
rule of softening the bust through scoop or asymmetric necklines, fluid drape,
vertical-line breaks. Filter sweetheart and bust-emphasis cuts.
● Inverted triangle + smaller bust: halter unlocks fully as a hero (small bust + halter
elongates the upper body and draws attention to collarbones without emphasizing
shoulder line). For fuller bust, halter still works but requires more careful selection.

Body + variation modulators:
● Athletic inverted triangle (developed shoulders, lean): avoid structured shoulders on
outerwear entirely. Soft drape and rounded shoulders on jackets. Avoid cap sleeves that
emphasize delts.
● Bust-forward inverted triangle: deeper V-necks (medium depth, not deep unless
featuring) work to break the upper body; sweetheart works only as opt-in edge featuring.
Avoid bust-emphasis details by default.
● Narrow-frame inverted triangle: more styling tolerance. Can wear some items that
broader inverted triangles can't (cap sleeves on a very lean frame are less amplifying
because there's less bust/chest volume below them).
● Hip-fuller inverted triangle: suppress "add hip volume" defaults. Surface items that
balance through waist creation rather than added volume.

Hero dress and skirt silhouettes:

8. Disqualified Categories
Items that don't work, regardless of strategy:
● Structured shoulder pads
● Puff sleeves and dramatic sleeve volume at the shoulder
● Padded or push-up bust
● Off-shoulder, bateau, Sabrina necklines
● Boat necks
● Wide square necklines on tops
● Tight column dresses
● Statement collars and embellished necklines
● Bold prints or detail on tops without compensation on bottom
● Drop-waist dresses (extends upper body, shortens leg)
● Empire-line tops or dresses that emphasize the upper body
● Bandeau and strapless tops as default (removes strap interruption, broadens visual
shoulder)

● Mermaid skirts that are tight to the knee then flare (the wrong tight section emphasizes
narrow lower body)
● Double-breasted jackets (adds upper-body volume)
Items disqualified by specific answer combinations:
● Short skirts and dresses disqualified for: inverted triangle + legs covered
● Wide-leg pants disqualified for: inverted triangle + very narrow-frame + close fit
preference (where the wide-leg becomes disproportionate)
● Backless items disqualified for: inverted triangle + back covered
● Sleeveless items disqualified for: inverted triangle + arms covered (cap sleeves or short
sleeves substitute)
● Sweetheart and deep-V disqualified for: inverted triangle + chest covered or bust
signaled to minimize
Items that look right but disappoint:
● Crew-neck T-shirts on inverted triangle: mainstream basics, look fine on the hanger, but
the closed neckline + fitted ribcage on a broad upper body reads as boxy and amplifies
what's already there. Surface V-necks and scoop necks instead.
● Boxy crewneck sweaters: "oversized" item that fails on inverted triangle through both
fronts — closed neck and shoulder breadth amplified.
● Pencil skirts in stretchy fabric: market themselves as universally flattering, but on
inverted triangles the tight skirt on narrow hips emphasizes the proportion mismatch.
Pencil with structure and slight flare can work; bodycon pencil fails.
● Tank tops with wide straps or wide armholes: even sleeveless can fail if the strap
extends the shoulder line or exposes the full shoulder breadth without compensation.
9. The Uniform
Daytime / casual uniform (mid-height, mid-sophistication, mixed strategy):
A pair of high-rise wide-leg or straight-leg pants in mid or dark wash denim, or a tailored trouser
in neutral fabric, sitting at the natural waist with a clean break into the leg. Length is full or
near-full; ankle break is fine for casual.
On top, a fitted V-neck or scoop neck tank or knit, ending at the high hip. The neckline is open to
break the chest panel; the fit is close to skim the body without bulk. If layering, a soft drape
blouse or open button-down worn unbuttoned, sleeves rolled. The layer is in fluid fabric without
structured shoulders.

Optionally, a soft drape blazer or open cardigan as a third layer, worn open to create a vertical
line down the front. The blazer must not have shoulder pads or aggressively structured
construction.
If the outfit needs more anchor, a medium-width belt at the natural waist creates definition; a
wide statement belt risks shortening the torso.
Footwear: low-profile or with a heel that extends the leg. Pointed flats, ankle boots, simple
sandals, low heels for register.
Variations by strategy:
● Trace-primary inverted triangle: swap to fitted button-down (worn slightly open) with
straight-leg trouser. Less proportion play, more careful body skimming. Specific selection
because trace is restrictive for this body type.
● Anchor-primary inverted triangle: lead with wrap top + flared skirt, or fit-and-flare dress
as a single piece. Waist + lower body volume in one move.
● Sculpt-primary inverted triangle: swap separates for a fit-and-flare dress with structured
bodice and full skirt. Sculpt at the dress level rather than through layering.
● Edge-primary inverted triangle: short A-line dress for casual; halter top + wide-leg for
evening-leaning casual; statement-printed bottom + plain top for color play.
Variations by register:
● Work: swap to tailored trousers in wool or substantial cotton, fitted V-neck knit or
button-down worn open over a base layer, soft-shoulder blazer.
● Evening: fit-and-flare dresses, wrap dresses, halter dresses (especially with full skirt
below), fluid drape gowns with defined waist. Avoid heavy structured shoulder gowns
and bandeau silhouettes.
● Occasion: full A-line dresses, fit-and-flare midi, wrap dresses are universally appropriate.
Statement skirts with simple tops for cocktail.
Variations by season:
● Summer: wide-leg cotton or linen trousers, V-neck tanks, halter tops, sleeveless wrap
dresses, A-line short skirts, fit-and-flare sundresses, soft drape sleeveless blouses, soft
sandals.
● Winter: wide-leg wool trousers, fine knit V-necks tucked in, soft drape cashmere, trench
or wrap coats with belt, knee-length A-line wool coats, knee-high boots paired with skirts
(the boots extend the leg visually).
10. Inference Notes for the Recommender

Default rules that fire on inverted triangle identification alone:
1. Lower-body volume is a goal; surface items that add visual hip volume (A-line, full,
flared, wide-leg).
2. Upper-body softening is a goal; filter or downrank structured shoulders, shoulder pads,
puff sleeves.
3. Waist creation is a primary tool; surface anchor items (wrap, peplum, fit-and-flare,
belted) for any user not explicitly rejecting them.
4. Legs are an asset by default; short lengths and slits available unless explicitly
suppressed.
5. Open necklines are a default relief move for the upper body; surface V, scoop, halter,
asymmetric, open button placket. Filter boat neck, wide square,
off-shoulder/bateau/Sabrina.
6. Closed crew necks on tops downweighted; the standard tee category needs to be V or
scoop instead.
7. Bulk on top is a risk; thin fluid drape preferred over thick structured fabric.
8. Bulk on bottom is welcome; structured A-line, full skirts, wide-leg pants are heroes.
9. Soft fabric on top, structured on bottom is the default fabric preference inverse of
rectangle.
10. Bottom-up attention is the goal; surface items that draw the eye to legs, waist, or lower
body.
11. Sleeves vary by strategy and body variation: cap sleeves work for narrow-frame, fail for
athletic and bust-forward; bishop and bell sleeves work universally.
12. Athletic variation modifies sleeve and shoulder defaults significantly — surface softer
shoulders aggressively.
13. Standard pant lengths may run wrong at height extremes; surface tall and petite cuts
where available.
14. Petite + inverted triangle: shoulder breadth is harder to balance on smaller frame.
Surface controlled bottom volume (not overwhelming) and shorter A-line skirts that show
legs.
15. Bust-as-edge featuring requires explicit opt-in via combination signals (fuller bust + chest
exposure comfortable + behavior signaling bust to feature). Default position is bust
minimization.
Modulators that change defaults:
● Strategy selection: anchor strongly preferred and weighted up; sculpt requires waist +
lower volume; edge unlocks featuring (legs, décolletage, ankle by default; bust on opt-in)
and attention-redirect modes; trace requires hero-weighted surfacing.
● Fit preferences: close top + relaxed bottom is the natural inverted triangle setup; relaxed
top + close bottom fights the body and requires careful filtering. Close-everywhere
preference flags skinny/slim bottoms as user-preferred despite default low priority.
● Fabric importance: high importance + structured items → strict construction filter; high
importance + soft drape → quality drape filter.

● Exposure modulates asset access: chest covered constrains necklines significantly;
arms covered limits sleeveless options; legs covered removes the primary balancing
asset (significant constraint).
● Bust signal modulates featuring access: default is minimization; explicit opt-in via
combination unlocks featuring as edge move.
Sophistication and restrictiveness signals:
Same framework as rectangle:
● Multi-strategy + flexible answers → widen catalog
● Trace-only + multiple covered zones + petite → tighten catalog, hero-weight, fewer items
higher quality
Risk-scanning patterns common to inverted triangle:
● How tops sit at the shoulder (does fabric pull or pucker, do button-downs gap at the
chest)
● Whether a sleeve adds shoulder width through cut or padding
● Whether a top's volume sits at the bust or the hip (latter is better for IT)
● Whether bottoms have enough volume to balance the top
● How pants fit at the waist when they fit at the hip (universal narrow-hip complaint)
Common "knows the trick" non-traditional moves:
● Sophisticated inverted triangles may wear strong horizontal interest at the hip (cargo
pockets, hip detail, dropped yoke) as deliberate hip-volume creation.
● May wear long full skirts as a fashion-forward balancing move when most users wear
pants.
● May use color blocking with dark on top, bright on bottom to redirect attention downward
(outfit-level edge logic).
● May feature bust intentionally with sweetheart or surplice cuts when bust is the preferred
feature rather than the proportion problem.
On rule restrictiveness vs. user variation:
Same principle as rectangle: rules optimize for the population of inverted triangle users, not
every variation within. Some inverted triangles look great in items the rules filter out (heavily
structured shoulders on a confident athletic user; off-shoulder dresses on a user who specifically
wants to feature shoulders). The rules correctly don't surface these proactively because the cost
of mis-surfacing them to the median inverted triangle is higher than the cost of missing them for
variation users.

11. Open Questions and Edge Cases
Cases the rules don't yet handle:
● Inverted triangle + apple borderline: user with shoulder breadth and midsection width.
Both rule sets fire partially; need to determine which dominates. Probably: surface waist
creation aggressively (both body types benefit), filter detail on top (both body types
penalize), but suppress hip-volume defaults (apple has midsection volume rather than
narrow-hip problem).
● Inverted triangle + hourglass with broad shoulders: user with defined waist AND broad
shoulders. Hourglass rules mostly apply (honor the natural shape) but with caution about
emphasizing shoulders further.
● Inverted triangle with heavy bust: when chest is the more dominant feature than shoulder
width. May need separate bust-focused rule set rather than treating as shoulder-driven.
● Inverted triangle who hates her shoulders: behavioral signal that should suppress most
upper-body emphasis even when chest is technically uncovered. Learn from search and
rejection patterns.
Borderline body-type identifications:
● Inverted triangle vs. rectangle with broad shoulders: if shoulders are 1-3% wider than
hips, treat as rectangle (rules will mostly serve). If 5%+ wider, treat as inverted triangle.
● Athletic build vs. inverted triangle: essentially the same body type for styling purposes.
Both benefit from inverted triangle rules.
● Pear with strong shoulders: if hips are broader than shoulders but shoulders are still
developed, treat as pear (pear rules dominate). Pears with developed shoulders can use
some inverted triangle softening moves on the upper body.
Things behavior data should resolve:
● Color preferences (dark on top vs. bottom is a common inverted triangle move; learn
whether individual users do this)
● Specific brand affinities (some brands cut tops with more shoulder room; learn which)
● Tolerance for hip-volume intensity (some users want subtle A-line; others want dramatic
full skirt)
● Whether the user wants to emphasize or downplay shoulders (sometimes hard to extract
from questionnaire; learn from behavior)
● Sleeve volume tolerance (bishop and bell are heroes but volume varies)
● Skinny jeans preference (default low priority, but learn whether individual users prefer
them and promote accordingly)
● Bust featuring preference (default minimization, but learn whether individual users opt in
via behavior even when questionnaire is ambiguous)

Body Type: Hourglass
Body Type: Hourglass
1. Identification
How users self-identify in Q2: "I have a defined waist with balanced bust and hips."
Confirming signals from Q3 (distribution):
Snug zones — diagnostic:
● Chest and hips/seat together (signals balanced volume top and bottom)
● Hips/thighs (balanced curves through the lower half)
● Seat (hourglass typically has shape at the seat)
● Chest only (full-bust hourglass)
Snug zones — noisy / not diagnostic:
● Waist/stomach (rarely snug for hourglass; if reported, suggests midsection-prone
variation rather than primary signal)
● Arms (full-bust and soft hourglass users often gain weight in arms along with
bust/hips/thighs; reporting arms snug doesn't reclassify the body type but informs fabric
and fit modulators — looser sleeves, stretch in sleeve fabric, fitted set-in sleeves that
follow the arm without pulling)
● Shoulders (rarely diagnostic; if reported with snug bust, may indicate top-hourglass
variation)
Loose zones — diagnostic:
● Waist/stomach (the universal hourglass signal — defined waist between curves)
● "Nowhere specific" (possible for slimmer hourglass where overall fit is workable but waist
still defined)
Loose zones — noisy:
● Arms (not body-shape diagnostic on its own)
Confirming signals from Q4 (fit issues):

Fit issues — diagnostic:
● Pants gap at the back waistband (the universal hourglass complaint — small waist +
curvy hips)
● Tops pull across the bust but are loose at the waist
● Button-downs gape at the bust
● Buying tops one size up to fit bust → loose and boxy elsewhere
● Dresses fit waist but pull at bust, or fit bust but loose at waist
● Coats are top-heavy when sized for bust
● "Everything usually sits how it should" — possible for petite hourglass at lower volume or
for users who've already adapted their buying to curvy fits
Length issues — diagnostic:
● Tops feel too cropped → if tall
● Pants feel too short → if tall
● Pants feel too long → if petite
● "Everything usually sits where it should" — common for mid-height hourglass around
162–172 cm
Conflicting or ambiguous signals to watch for:
● Snug at waist: unusual for hourglass. If reported, consider hourglass + midsection-prone
variation (post-pregnancy, post-menopause, or natural soft midsection) — the
proportions are still hourglass but the waist is softer than peak definition.
● Loose at hips and seat: possible for slimmer hourglass or top-hourglass variation. Don't
reclassify; check whether bust/hip width ratio still shows balance.
● Loose at chest with defined waist: small-bust hourglass. Shoulders and hips are
balanced, waist is defined, bust is just smaller in absolute terms. Still hourglass —
proportion is what defines the type, not absolute bust size.
● Snug at chest and hips, loose at waist, but with a flat seat: possibly inverted triangle with
defined waist, not true hourglass. Check whether the seat has curve or is flat.
Distinctions from adjacent body types:
● Hourglass vs. rectangle: hourglass has measurable waist taper (waist visibly smaller
than bust and hips). Rectangle has minimal or no taper. The Q3 + Q4 combination (loose
at waist, snug at chest and hips, pants gap at back) reliably distinguishes.
● Hourglass vs. inverted triangle: hourglass has balanced shoulders and hips. Inverted
triangle has shoulders distinctly broader than hips. If the user says shoulders are
broadest, it's not hourglass.
● Hourglass vs. pear: hourglass has balanced bust and hips. Pear has hips wider than
bust/shoulders. The bust signal is the differentiator.
● Hourglass vs. apple: apple has widest point at the midsection. Hourglass has the
narrowest point at the midsection. Direct opposite signals.

● Hourglass vs. top hourglass vs. bottom hourglass: mild proportion variations within
hourglass. If bust is slightly larger than hips → top hourglass (leans toward inverted
triangle but waist is still defined). If hips are slightly larger than bust → bottom hourglass
(leans toward pear but waist is still defined). Hourglass rules still apply; pear or inverted
triangle modulations may help at edges.
2. Geometric Truth
Core proportions:
● Bust and hips approximately equal in width (within ~5%)
● Waist visibly narrower than both bust and hips (typically 25%+ smaller)
● Shoulders typically aligned with hip width (sometimes slightly narrower than bust
depending on variation)
● Seat has shape and projection from the side (not flat)
● Curves are gradual and smooth, not angular
Where volume sits:
● Bust, hips, seat, thighs — distributed evenly between top and bottom
● Waist and stomach are the narrowest zones
● Arms and legs are usually proportional, sometimes soft in soft-hourglass variation
Length tendencies:
● Variable — no consistent torso/leg ratio across hourglass users
● Waist position can be high or low; affects how garments hit
● Petite hourglass often has shorter torso relative to legs
Common geometric variations within the type:
● Lean/fit hourglass: defined waist, balanced bust/hip, muscle definition with low softness.
Body holds shape on its own. Can carry body-skimming fits (bodycon, fitted column, slim
trousers) without fabric pulling or revealing texture. Stretch matters less; stable wovens
work.
● Soft/fleshy hourglass: same proportions as lean hourglass but with naturally soft
musculature. Body has give. Stretch fabrics become more important — they follow
curves without revealing every texture. Bodycon may or may not work depending on
individual comfort with how fabric reads on soft tissue. Tailoring is critical because
off-the-rack often pulls in wrong places.
● Top hourglass: bust slightly larger than hips, shoulders slightly broader. Hourglass rules
apply but with some inverted-triangle considerations (V-necks and open necklines work
especially well to break up the upper body; avoid further shoulder volume).

● Bottom hourglass: hips slightly larger than bust. Hourglass rules apply but with some
pear considerations (waist-marking remains critical; can balance volume slightly toward
the upper body when needed).
● Petite hourglass: same proportions at smaller scale. Overwhelmed easily by volume.
Needs careful proportion in every piece — peplum that hits at natural waist (not below),
short jackets that end at waist (not hip), proportional accessories.
● Midsection-prone hourglass: waist proportionally still smaller than bust/hips but less
sharply defined than peak. Common after pregnancy, post-menopause, or natural body
composition shift. Styling still emphasizes waist illusion through cut, tucking, layering,
and belt placement but with more coverage in the midsection.
3. Visual Assets (default, before strategy modulation)
Natural assets (read well without intervention):
● Defined waist: the central asset for hourglass. The narrowest visible point of the body.
Most styling decisions revolve around showing or honoring this point.
● Balanced proportions: the bust and hips work together to frame the waist. Both are
assets when shown in balance.
● Curves overall: unlike rectangle (where shape needs to be created) or inverted triangle
(where balance needs to be created), hourglass has the shape already. The body reads
as feminine and balanced without external help.
● Décolletage and bust line: for full-bust hourglass, the bust itself is often a feature; for
smaller-bust hourglass, the collarbone area reads as elegant.
● Seat and hip curve: the lower body has natural shape that high-rise bottoms emphasize.
● Legs: often shapely; not necessarily long but typically have feminine curve.
Features that become assets when used intentionally:
● Lengthening the silhouette: vertical lines, monochromatic outfits, and pointed shoes can
elongate the body, which is useful because curves naturally read as adding visual width.
● Waist precision: belts, high-rise meeting points, and tailored seams can make the natural
waist look even smaller.
● Hip emphasis: some hourglass users prefer to emphasize hips further as a styling
choice. Low-rise bottoms, hip-detail items, and skirt cuts that widen at the hip serve this
preference — not a default but a real opt-in.
● Amplified curves: sculpt items (corsets, fit-and-flare with very fitted bodice + dramatic
flare, princess-seam construction) can exaggerate the natural waist-to-hip ratio for users
who want more pronounced shape than their body shows.
Override conditions:

● Bust not for featuring (with cleavage): default for full-bust users at low or medium
exposure preference. Moderate open necklines work; cleavage-revealing depth doesn't.
See section 6 for combinational handling.
● Curves to be minimized: if user signals discomfort with curve emphasis (sometimes seen
post-pregnancy or in conservative-dress contexts). Surface darker colors, vertical lines,
structured tailoring rather than body-skimming. Default body-skimming preference
softens; still avoid boxy/oversized which is universally bad.
● Hips/seat not for featuring: if user covers lower body extensively or signals discomfort
with hip emphasis. Bottom hourglass variation often falls here. Default high-rise still
applies; pencil skirts and bodycon get downweighted; peplum and sculpt-amplification
get filtered.
● Hip emphasis preferred: opt-in for users who want fuller hip silhouette. Low-rise
becomes available; peplum and sculpt that exaggerate waist-hip ratio become heroes.
4. Visual Risks (default, before strategy modulation)
Silhouettes that fight this body type:
● Boxy, shapeless tops: the #1 universal hourglass risk. T-shirts, sweatshirts, tunics that
hang straight from bust to hip hide the waist and add visual width by making the body
read as a column.
● Oversized cuts: same problem — fabric volume covers the waist asset. Includes
oversized blazers, boyfriend cuts without belt, sack dresses, trapeze cuts.
● Drop-waist dresses: drops the visual waist below the natural waist, eliminating the asset
and creating a top-heavy/bottom-heavy reading depending on cut.
● Empire-waist dresses and tops: places the seam above the natural waist, hiding the
actual waist and creating a column from the bust down.
● Shift dresses: straight cut from shoulder to hem hides the entire waist line.
● Tent and trapeze cuts: same problem at increased volume.
● Low-rise bottoms: sit at the widest hip point, widening visual hip line. Not a default failure
— some hourglass users explicitly want this effect. Available by default at neutral priority;
downrank only when user signals high/mid-rise preference; promote when user signals
hip emphasis or close-to-hip preference at the waist.
● Pleats at the hip or stomach: add bulk where the body is narrowest.
● Paperbag waists: the gathered fabric at the waist adds bulk to the body's narrowest
zone, doing the opposite of what hourglass needs.
● Heavy gathered skirts at the waist: same issue.
● Mini skirts (sometimes): can disrupt proportion by drawing the eye to the hip line. Not
universal — works on lean hourglass with proportional legs; risky on soft hourglass.
● Stiff fabrics that don't drape on the body: the fabric stands away from the waist instead of
following it, creating a column effect.

● Cropped tops that hit at the natural waist or above: can cut off the silhouette mid-curve in
a way that emphasizes hip width below.
● Long, heavy outerwear and tops: large overcoat lengths, long boxy cardigans, long
sweaters that hang past the hip — these can drown hourglass. The eye loses the waist
meeting point and the body reads as a column from shoulder to knee. Length itself isn't
the issue; length without waist visibility is. Long pieces work when they're belted, worn
open over a fitted base, or constructed with waist shaping. Long pieces in heavy fabric
without these compensations are higher-risk than equivalent rectangle or inverted
triangle versions.
Construction details that fail:
● Embellishments at the bust, shoulders, or hips: add bulk to zones that are already
visually substantial.
● Big buttons or double-breasted construction down the front: visual clutter that distracts
from the waist and adds chest weight.
● Patch pockets at the hip: adds width at hip line.
● Strong shoulder pads: throws off the balance, makes shoulders read broader than hips.
● Heavy front bulk on coats: large lapels, big buttons, toggle closures — all visual weight
at the bust that fights the waist.
Fabric behaviors that fail:
● Stiff structured fabrics (heavy canvas, thick wool that holds shape rigidly): don't follow
the body's curves. Better for inverted triangle or rectangle; bad for hourglass who needs
fabric that drapes to the natural shape.
● Thick, bulky knits: add visual weight everywhere; hide waist; make curves read as bulk.
● Crispness without give: tops cut for proportional bodies pull at the bust because there's
no stretch to accommodate.
● Heavy embellishment that adds visible weight: sequins, beading, heavy embroidery
placed at the bust or hip create bulk.
Necklines that fail (default):
● For full-bust hourglass at low/medium exposure: cleavage-revealing depths; deep V
plunging below mid-chest; low sweetheart that exposes substantial cleavage.
● For full-bust hourglass worn fitted without compensation: high closed crew, mock neck,
turtleneck (add visual chest weight when fabric clings to bust); wide square or boat neck
(broaden shoulders and add upper-body width).
● For small-bust hourglass: failures are mostly bust-specific and don't apply; nearly all
necklines accessible.
Length traps:

● For petite hourglass: standard midi skirts often hit at calf-widest, shortening and adding
width; standard mini skirts can read too short proportionally; below-knee or midi-with-slit
works better.
● For tall hourglass: standard tops often too short; standard pants too short; needs to look
for tall sizing.
Override conditions:
● Oversized tops: can work when (a) worn with a slim/fitted bottom to maintain proportion,
(b) tucked or half-tucked to restore waist visibility, or (c) belted to recreate the waist.
Outright shapeless without compensation = universal hourglass failure.
● Cap sleeves: generally fine but balance with proportional bottom volume; if upper body
already feels emphasized, downweight.
● High closed neckline on full-bust hourglass: works when (a) the fabric is relaxed and
doesn't cling to bust, (b) the top is tucked/belted with clear waist visibility, or (c) layered
open with a fitted base. The bust shelf reading only happens when closed neckline +
fitted bust fabric + no waist compensation combine.
5. Strategy Modulation
Trace
What trace means for this body type: The dominant and most natural strategy for hourglass.
The body already has the shape; clothes need only to follow it. Unlike rectangle (where trace
traces a column and fails) or inverted triangle (where trace traces the proportion mismatch),
trace on hourglass traces curves the body already has, which is exactly what styling literature
recommends. The vast majority of hourglass hero items are trace items — fitted blouses, knit
tops, pencil skirts, slim pants, sheath dresses, wrap items in fitted cuts.
What works under trace:
● Fitted knits and ribbed tops
● Wrap tops and blouses (cinch waist while following bust)
● Fitted button-downs (when sized correctly — see fit issues)
● Pencil skirts (knee or longer)
● Slim trousers and tapered pants
● High-rise straight or slim jeans (curvy fit when available)
● Sheath dresses (knee or longer)
● Bodycon dresses in structured stretch fabric (for users comfortable with bodycon)
● Fit-and-flare dresses (fit at top, flare at hip)
● Fitted blazers with shape at the waist
● Cropped jackets ending at the natural waist

What fails under trace:
● Trace becomes "cling" if fabric is too thin or thin-stretchy on soft hourglass — shows
every texture
● Bodycon in cheap stretch can pull badly at curves
● Thin viscose t-shirts can read clingy in the wrong places
● Long, heavy fitted pieces (long structured coats, long fitted cardigans) can drag down
the silhouette and lose the waist meeting point — shorter and proportional works better
than long and heavy
Special considerations:
● Sized for hips, tailored at the waist — almost universal for hourglass off-the-rack tops
and dresses
● For lean hourglass: any reasonable trace fabric works
● For soft hourglass: stretch and structured stretch is critical; pure stiff wovens fight the
body, pure thin jerseys cling too closely
● Cropped and short pieces work better than long fitted pieces across hourglass, not just
for petite — the shorter length keeps the waist meeting point visible
Anchor
What anchor means for this body type: A core hourglass strategy — possibly the most
universally useful one in real-world dressing. Hourglass anchor doesn't create a waist (the body
has one); it makes the existing waist more visible through cuts, construction, or styling that
draws the eye to it. Critically, anchor is the bridge strategy for hourglass users who don't want to
wear tight or body-skimming clothes but still want their waist asset visible. Belt over a relaxed
top, half-tuck on an oversized shirt, drawstring waist on a loose dress — all anchor moves that
preserve the waist's visibility without requiring fitted clothing.
Hard vs. soft anchor: Both work universally for hourglass. The body has the waist; almost any
anchor mechanism (belt, drawstring, wrap, defined waist seam, tuck) succeeds because there's
real waist underneath. This is the opposite of rectangle (where soft anchor fails because no
waist underneath) or inverted triangle (where anchor must do real silhouette work in addition to
existing). Hourglass anchor is permissive — almost everything counts.
Specific anchor moves that work:
● Wrap dresses and wrap tops
● Belted dresses (belt at natural waist)
● Belted shirt dresses
● Belted blazers and coats
● Tucked-in tops (full tuck, half tuck, French tuck — all preserve waist visibility)
● High-rise bottoms (creates implicit anchor by meeting at the natural waist)
● Dresses with built-in waist seams that match natural waist

● Drawstring or self-tie tops in fitted or relaxed cuts
● Statement belts over relaxed tops or coats
● Cinched-waist construction (smocking, elastic at waist, gathered side detail)
Anchor moves that fail:
● Drop-waist anything (drops the visual waist below natural)
● Empire-waist anything (raises the visual waist above natural)
● Cinches at the wrong waist position (paperbag bunching above natural waist)
● Wide belts that cover too much torso visually (especially on petite)
● Belts placed at the hip rather than the waist
● Thin string belts on heavy fabric (don't actually cinch — purely decorative)
Special note on anchor for relaxed-fit hourglass: This is where anchor earns its place. A
user who picks relaxed top + relaxed bottom but is hourglass and wants her shape preserved
needs anchor as her primary mechanism. Surface belts, tucking-friendly cuts (clean hem, not
too long, fabric that tucks without bulk), wrap tops worn loose, drawstring waist dresses. These
items maintain the relaxed feel while preserving waist visibility.
Sculpt
What sculpt means for this body type: For hourglass, sculpt isn't about creating shape that
doesn't exist — it's about amplifying the existing waist-to-hip ratio. The body has the shape;
sculpt makes it more dramatic. This is a styling choice (user wants more pronounced curves
than her natural body shows) rather than a body need. Surface sculpt to users who signal
preference for exaggerated curve emphasis; don't push it on users who simply want to honor
their natural shape (trace handles that).
What good sculpt looks like for this body:
● Corseted bodice dresses (dramatic waist cinching beyond natural)
● Fit-and-flare with very fitted bodice + dramatic skirt flare (exaggerates the bodice-to-skirt
transition)
● Princess-seamed dresses that pull in at the waist more sharply than the body's natural
taper
● Peplum tops and jackets (creates flared hip silhouette below the waist, amplifying
waist-to-hip contrast)
● Tailored fit-and-flare coats with strong waist shaping
● Bustier-style dresses with full skirts
Peplum specifically: A sculpt item, not anchor. Peplum creates a hip flare that may or may not
match the body's actual proportions. For lean hourglass with balanced hip volume, peplum adds
hip width that isn't really there — creating a more exaggerated curve, which is a sculpt choice.
Filter peplum for petite hourglass (often hits at hip-widest in a way that visually doubles the hip
line — unless very short peplum hitting precisely at the natural waist), bottom hourglass

(already-fuller hips don't need amplification), and users signaling hip minimization. Peplum is a
hero for users who want amplified curves and have a clear natural waist for the peplum to flare
from.
What bad sculpt looks like:
● Sculpt with structured shoulders (adds upper-body weight, breaks balance)
● Sculpt in stiff fabric that fights the body's give (especially soft hourglass)
● Sculpt with embellishment that adds bulk to bust or hip
● Sculpt that imposes a waist different from the user's actual waist position (high-empire
sculpt on a body with natural waist creates costume effect)
● Peplum that hits below the natural waist or at the hip-widest point
Special considerations:
● Sculpt is more useful for midsection-prone hourglass who needs structural help
maintaining the waist illusion — but framed as anchor support, not curve amplification
● For lean hourglass who already has dramatic natural curves, sculpt is often
over-engineering; trace + anchor do the work
● For users who want a more retro/pin-up aesthetic, sculpt is the strategy that delivers it
● For users who want a softer or more minimal look, sculpt feels too constructed
Edge
What edge means for this body type: Edge for hourglass operates differently than for
rectangle or inverted triangle. The body's silhouette is already balanced and the waist is already
the focal point — there's no proportion problem to redirect attention away from. Edge for
hourglass is mostly about:
● Featuring specific assets: décolletage (for those with bust at appropriate exposure), legs
(when proportional), waist itself (through dramatic emphasis)
● Lengthening the silhouette: using vertical lines, monochromatic blocking, and pointed
shoes to elongate (because curves read as adding visual width, lengthening
counterbalances)
● Maintaining balance when introducing volume in one zone: if the user wants a
voluminous skirt, balance with a fitted top; if she wants a billowy sleeve, pair with a slim
bottom — edge as proportion management
Featuring modes:
● Bust featuring: deep V, sweetheart, surplice (when chest exposure permitted at
appropriate depth — see section 6)
● Leg featuring: shorter hems with slim/proportional fit elsewhere
● Waist featuring: very fitted waist seams, statement belts, color-blocked waist

● Décolletage: open necklines that show the upper chest without crossing into bust
dominance
● Hip featuring: low-rise bottoms, hip-detail items, skirts that widen at the hip — opt-in for
users who want hip emphasis
Balance modes:
● Volume on bottom + fitted top (preserves waist visibility while allowing fashion-forward
bottom volume)
● Volume on top + fitted bottom (less common for hourglass; works when the top is belted
or has waist-fitting at the bottom hem)
● Monochromatic outfits to elongate
Edge moves that fail:
● Edge moves that hide the waist (defeats the body's primary asset)
● Edge through oversized layering (hides curves under fabric)
● Statement pieces at bust + hip simultaneously without waist emphasis
6. Combinational Rules
Body + strategy combinations:
● Hourglass + trace: surface trace items aggressively. This is the body's strongest match.
Both lean and soft variations work; soft hourglass needs more stretch.
● Hourglass + anchor: surface belted, wrap, peplum-when-appropriate, tucked styles.
Critical bridge strategy for relaxed-fit users — surface tucking-friendly tops, belts,
drawstring waists, wrap tops worn loose. Not optional support; often primary mechanism.
● Hourglass + sculpt: surface sculpt that follows existing curves and amplifies them; filter
sculpt that imposes shape, adds upper-body volume, or fits in stiff fabric that fights the
body. Gated by user signal for amplified curves.
● Hourglass + edge: surface featuring moves (bust at appropriate exposure, legs, waist,
hips for opt-in users) and balance moves (volume on one zone with fitted compensation).
Filter edge moves that hide the waist.
● Hourglass + trace only: the body's natural fit; no special restrictiveness needed.
● Hourglass + multiple strategies: widen catalog; sophisticated hourglass dressers can do
almost anything that maintains waist visibility.
Body + fit combinations:
● Hourglass + close everywhere: preferred default. Almost all hero items fit this pattern.

● Hourglass + close top + relaxed bottom: works when relaxed bottom is structured
(wide-leg trousers with crisp construction) or tucked. Fails when relaxed bottom is
shapeless and waist hidden.
● Hourglass + relaxed top + close bottom: requires the relaxed top to be belted, tucked,
half-tucked, or worn open over fitted base layer. Otherwise hides the waist.
● Hourglass + relaxed everywhere: requires compensation through belt, tuck, or structure
somewhere. Pure shapeless = universal failure. This is the anchor-bridge case —
surface anchor items as the primary mechanism for maintaining waist visibility.
Body + fabric combinations:
● Lean hourglass + fabric flexibility: most fabrics work. Stable wovens, knits, stretch — all
serve.
● Soft hourglass + stretch importance: stretch fabrics weighted up significantly. Pure stiff
wovens deprioritized unless tailored. Thin clingy fabrics caution-flagged.
● Hourglass + cling-averse: filter thin jerseys and slinky polyesters. Surface ponte,
structured stretch, ribbed knits, stable wovens with give.
● Hourglass + stiff-fabric-averse: filter heavy canvas, thick stiff wools, rigid construction.
Surface drapey fabrics that follow curves.
Body + bust + exposure combinations:
The bust + exposure intersection matters significantly for hourglass and current questionnaire
signals don't always resolve it cleanly. Default rules:
● Hourglass + full bust + high exposure preference: deep V, plunging, low sweetheart,
surplice with deep depth all surfaced. Cleavage-revealing necklines available. Open
back accessible.
● Hourglass + full bust + medium exposure preference + chest not flagged as covered:
moderate-depth open necklines surfaced (V at collarbone-to-mid-chest, scoop at clavicle
area, wrap with moderate crossover). Deep V, plunging, and low sweetheart filtered. The
ambiguity here is real — she wants open necklines but not cleavage. Surface moderate;
let deeper styles emerge through behavior or search.
● Hourglass + full bust + medium exposure + chest flagged covered: moderate open
necklines still surface but higher-cut versions only (V ending above the bust line; scoop
at the collarbone; wrap with shallow crossover). Cleavage-revealing styles filtered.
● Hourglass + full bust + low exposure: surface higher slim necklines — V that ends above
the bust line, scoop at the collarbone, button-up with placket, mock neck with slim cut.
Layering items become important. Filter wide and deep cuts.
● Hourglass + small bust + any exposure: nearly all necklines accessible including crew,
mock, turtleneck, boat neck. Bust doesn't drive neckline restrictions. User preference
dominates.
● Hourglass + average bust: most necklines work; bust-emphasis necklines (sweetheart,
deep V) only when explicitly preferred via behavior or styling signal.

Follow-up question candidate: for full-bust hourglass at medium exposure, a refining question
with visual examples would resolve the ambiguity. Three options: "I prefer the neckline to sit
near my collarbones," "I prefer mid-chest depth," "I'm comfortable showing some cleavage."
This is a small additional question that significantly improves recommendation accuracy. Worth
adding to the questionnaire when full bust + medium exposure fires.
Body + neckline general principle:
The open-neckline preference for full bust is about bust-volume management, not a universal
hourglass rule. Small-bust and average-bust hourglass can wear any neckline including crew,
mock, and turtleneck. Full-bust hourglass can wear closed necklines (crew, mock) when the rest
of the outfit creates clear waist visibility through tuck, belt, or fitted bottom — the neckline isn't
the only failure point. Filter closed necklines for full-bust only when the closed neckline
combines with other bust-amplifying or waist-hiding factors. A relaxed crew-neck tee tucked into
high-rise jeans is a hero for hourglass regardless of bust size, because the tuck handles the
waist visibility and the closed neckline doesn't add bust bulk (relaxed fabric, not clinging).
Body + exposure combinations:
● Hourglass + chest covered: significant constraint for full-bust users. Surface higher slim
necklines with vertical line breaks; layering over a base; soft drape that doesn't add
chest bulk.
● Hourglass + legs uncovered: shorter hems available for users with proportional legs; not
required.
● Hourglass + waist covered: contradicts the body's primary asset. Surface items that hint
at waist (slim cut, vertical seams, color blocking that suggests cinching) without exposing
skin.
● Hourglass + back uncovered + edge: open backs accessible for evening.
Body + length and outerwear combinations:
● Hourglass + long outerwear: surface only when the outerwear has waist shaping, comes
belted, or has structural features that preserve waist visibility (princess seams, fitted at
waist + flare below, etc.). Filter long boxy coats, long oversized cardigans, long sweater
coats without waist shaping. This applies more strongly than the basic boxy filter — long
+ boxy on hourglass is a compound failure.
● Hourglass + short cropped jacket + high-rise bottom: hero pattern. The short jacket ends
at the waist, the high-rise bottom continues above the waist line, creating clear waist
visibility. Particularly strong for petite hourglass but works across hourglass.
● Hourglass + long top + slim bottom: requires the long top to be belted, tucked, or worn
open over a fitted base. Without compensation, fails.
Body + variation modulators:
● Lean hourglass: most cuts work; surface bodycon and body-skimming aggressively if
comfort signals allow.

● Soft hourglass: surface structured stretch (ponte, knit, ribbed); filter pure stiff woven
without tailoring; surface drapey fabrics with structure.
● Top hourglass: lean toward inverted triangle-friendly necklines (V, scoop); avoid further
shoulder volume.
● Bottom hourglass: lean toward pear-friendly bottom volume; avoid hip-emphasizing
detail.
● Petite hourglass: surface short jackets ending at waist; downweight long jackets; surface
knee-or-just-above hems; cautious with maxi unless proportions work.
● Midsection-prone hourglass: surface items with structural waist support (sturdy ponte,
fit-and-flare with built-in structure); filter clingy thin fabrics at the midsection; surface
vertical lines and dark midsection panels.

8. Disqualified Categories
Items that don't work, regardless of strategy:
● Boxy oversized tops without belting or tucking
● Sack and trapeze dresses
● Drop-waist dresses
● Empire-waist dresses (raise the visual waist above natural)
● Shift dresses with no waist definition
● Paperbag-waist bottoms
● Coats with heavy front bulk and no waist (e.g., cocoon, oversized teddy)
● Long, heavy outerwear without waist shaping or belt
● Items with embellishment heavily concentrated at bust or hips
● Thick chunky knit sweaters that hang straight from bust to hip
● Stiff fabrics that don't drape on the body
● Mom jeans with very loose hip (defeats the curvy-fit need)
● Double-breasted blazers with no waist shaping
Items disqualified by specific answer combinations:
● Bodycon disqualified for: hourglass + cling-averse + soft hourglass variation; or
hourglass + wants to minimize curves
● Mini skirts disqualified for: hourglass + soft variation + legs covered preference

● Pencil skirts disqualified for: hourglass + hips/seat to minimize
● Turtlenecks and high crews fitted disqualified for: hourglass + full bust + worn without
waist-visible compensation
● Stretch fabrics deprioritized for: lean hourglass + structured-fabric preference
● Peplum disqualified for: petite hourglass (unless very short, at natural waist), bottom
hourglass, users signaling hip minimization
● Sculpt-amplification disqualified for: users signaling minimal/natural curve preference
rather than amplified
Items that look right but disappoint:
● Boyfriend blazers on hourglass: marketed as universally chic, but the boxy cut hides the
waist and adds visual width. Always pair with belt or wear with very fitted bottom; ideally
avoid.
● Boxy "cool girl" cropped t-shirts: the cropped length hits at waist but the boxy fit hides the
waist line. Either tuck or size down for fitted cropped fit.
● Oversized button-downs: hides the waist; sized up for bust = boxy elsewhere. Surface
curvy-fit button-downs instead.
● Trapeze dresses: often marketed as flattering for "all body types"; they hide the
hourglass waist and create a top-heavy reading.
● Heavily-pleated trousers (paperbag, pleated front): add hip bulk and gather at the waist;
defeats the body's narrowest zone.
9. The Uniform
Daytime / casual uniform (mid-height, mid-sophistication, mixed strategy):
A pair of high-rise curvy-fit straight or slim jeans, sitting at the natural waist with the back
waistband fitting without gapping. A fitted knit top (ribbed cotton, fine-gauge merino, ponte)
tucked in or half-tucked, showing the waist meeting point. OR a relaxed soft crew-neck tee or
knit tucked into the same jeans — the tuck creates waist visibility while the relaxed fabric stays
comfortable.
A fitted blazer or denim jacket as a third layer, worn open. The blazer has waist shaping or
comes belted. Length ends at the natural waist or slightly below — not cropped above the waist,
not long below the hip.
If she'd rather wear a dress: a wrap dress in soft drapey fabric or a fit-and-flare midi with a
defined waist. Knee or just-above-knee length for most users; midi for taller users.
Footwear: pointed flats, ankle boots with proportional shaft, low heels for register. The shoe
should not visually chop the leg (very ankle-strappy styles can shorten on petite).

Variations by strategy:
● Trace-primary hourglass: simple fitted pieces. Knit top + slim pant. Sheath dress alone.
● Anchor-primary hourglass: belted styles dominate. Wrap dress, belted blazer over fitted
base, statement waist treatments. For relaxed-fit anchor users: oversized shirt +
statement belt + slim or wide-leg bottom; loose dress + waist belt; relaxed knit +
half-tuck.
● Sculpt-primary hourglass: corseted fit-and-flare, princess-seamed structured pieces,
peplum top + pencil skirt — for users who want amplified curves.
● Edge-primary hourglass: deeper neckline showing décolletage (at exposure-appropriate
depth); statement waist belt; monochromatic column for elongation; short hem with
proportional fit for leg feature; low-rise bottoms for users signaling hip emphasis.
Variations by register:
● Work: fitted button-down (curvy fit) tucked into tailored trousers; pencil skirt with knit;
sheath dress with belted blazer; wrap dress in professional fabric.
● Evening: wrap dress, fit-and-flare cocktail, bodycon (if comfortable), corseted bodice for
special occasion. Avoid heavy structured shoulders.
● Occasion: belted shirt dresses, fit-and-flare midis. Hourglass-cut dresses are nearly
universally appropriate for occasion wear.
Variations by season:
● Summer: fitted tanks (V or scoop), wrap dresses in light fabric, fit-and-flare sundresses,
high-rise shorts (curvy fit) with tucked top.
● Winter: fitted knit dresses, ribbed sweaters tucked in, belted trench coats, fitted wool
coats with waist shaping, knee-high boots paired with knee-length skirts.
10. Inference Notes for the Recommender
Default rules that fire on hourglass identification alone:
1. Show the waist — central rule. Filter anything that hides it without compensation (belt,
tuck, fitted base layer).
2. Surface trace items aggressively. The body's natural shape is the asset.
3. Surface anchor items as bridge strategy for relaxed-fit hourglass users — belts,
tucking-friendly tops, wrap tops, drawstring waists. Anchor is not optional support; it's
primary mechanism for users who don't dress fitted.
4. Surface curvy-fit cuts when available. Standard tops cut for proportional bodies often fail.
5. High-rise bottoms preferred by default. Low-rise available; surface when user signals hip
emphasis or close-to-hip preference; downrank when user signals high/mid-rise
preference.

6. Stretch fabrics weighted up for soft hourglass; pure stiff wovens weighted down unless
tailored.
7. Bust-specific necklines: depth calibrated by bust + exposure combination, not by bust
alone. Closed necklines (crew, mock, turtleneck) accessible for full-bust hourglass when
worn tucked or belted with clear waist visibility elsewhere in the outfit.
8. Boxy and oversized items filtered unless paired with belt, tuck, or fitted compensation.
9. Drop-waist, empire-waist, shift, trapeze, tent all filtered by default.
10. Long heavy outerwear filtered by default unless belted or with waist shaping; long + boxy
is compound failure for hourglass.
11. Coats with heavy front bulk or no waist definition deprioritized.
12. Petite + hourglass: short jackets ending at waist; downweight long structured pieces;
cautious with peplum (only when short and at natural waist).
13. Sculpt surfaced for users who want amplified curves, not as universal need. Peplum is a
sculpt item, gated by hip preference and body height.
14. Soft variation + structured stretch (ponte, ribbed knit) weighted up; pure thin jerseys
downweighted; pure stiff woven downweighted unless tailored.
15. Lean variation + most fabrics tolerable.
16. Mini hem accessible for users with leg-uncovered preference and proportional legs;
downweight for soft hourglass or hip-emphasis-averse.
17. Arms-snug signal (common for fuller hourglass) modulates sleeve fabric and fit —
surface stretch sleeves, set-in fitted sleeves that follow arm without pulling.
Modulators that change defaults:
● Strategy selection: trace dominant; anchor as critical bridge for relaxed-fit users (not
optional); sculpt gated by curve-amplification signal; edge for proportion play, asset
featuring, and hip emphasis opt-in.
● Fit preferences: close everywhere is the natural default; relaxed needs anchor
compensation; relaxed + anchor is a hero combination, not a compromise.
● Fabric importance: high importance + structured items → tailoring or stretch with
structure; high importance + drape preferred → quality drape that follows curves without
clinging.
● Bust signal modulates neckline depth, not neckline shape. Closed necklines remain
accessible across bust sizes when waist visibility is handled elsewhere.
● Exposure signal modulates neckline depth more than shape. Full bust + medium
exposure surfaces moderate openness; full bust + low exposure surfaces high-but-slim
necklines.
● Variation (lean vs. soft) modulates fabric selection significantly.
● Hip emphasis preference unlocks low-rise as hero rather than risk.
Sophistication and restrictiveness signals:
Same framework as rectangle and inverted triangle:
● Multi-strategy + flexible answers → widen catalog

● Multiple covered zones + petite + soft hourglass → tighten catalog; surface fewer hero
items
● Curvy-fit availability matters more for restrictive users
Risk-scanning patterns common to hourglass:
● Whether tops pull at the bust (button gap, fabric pucker)
● Whether bottoms gap at the back waistband
● Whether construction adds bulk at bust or hip
● Whether the waist line is visible in the finished outfit
● Whether the fabric follows the body's curves or stands away from them
● Whether sleeve construction throws off shoulder/hip balance
● Whether peplum or sculpt items match user's curve-amplification preference or impose
unwanted exaggeration
Common "knows the trick" non-traditional moves:
● Sophisticated hourglass dressers may wear oversized items if they're consistently belted
or layered with a fitted base
● May wear bodycon as a polished evening piece for confident users
● May use color blocking strategically — dark waist panel within a lighter top to create
implied waist on relaxed cuts
● May wear menswear-inspired pieces if they're tailored with waist shaping (rare in retail;
usually requires alteration)
● May opt into low-rise bottoms specifically to emphasize hip-waist ratio further than
high-rise allows
● May choose oversized shirt + statement belt as primary anchor mechanism instead of
tucking
On rule restrictiveness vs. user variation:
Hourglass rules are remarkably consistent across styling sources. Unlike rectangle (where
execution matters more than rules) or inverted triangle (where balancing is contested),
hourglass has near-universal agreement on the core rule: show the waist. Variations within
hourglass (lean vs. soft, top vs. bottom, full bust vs. small bust, amplification preference vs.
natural preference) modulate execution but not the central principle.
This makes the hourglass rules easier to validate but also less forgiving — when an item fails for
hourglass, it usually fails clearly because it hides the waist. The system can be confident in
filtering shapeless items. Where it must remain permissive is on stylistic choices (low-rise for hip
emphasis, closed neckline with relaxed fabric and tuck) that some hourglass users genuinely
prefer.

11. Open Questions and Edge Cases
Cases the rules don't yet handle:
● Hourglass + post-pregnancy: waist often softer for several months/years; user may still
identify as hourglass by proportion but needs more structural support to maintain waist
illusion. Surface fit-and-flare with built-in shaping, structured ponte, supportive bodycon.
Filter thin clingy at midsection.
● Hourglass + post-menopause: same body type proportionally but with softer midsection.
Similar styling adjustments.
● Hourglass + pregnancy: not the same body type during pregnancy. Maternity wear has
its own rules.
● Hourglass + soft hourglass who's gained significant weight: may shift toward apple
temporarily if midsection becomes the widest point. Re-classify based on current
geometry, not historical body type.
● Hourglass + athletic build: the lean/fit hourglass variation captures this when athletic
without losing curves. Pure athletic without curves shifts toward rectangle or inverted
triangle.
Borderline body-type identifications:
● Hourglass vs. top hourglass leaning inverted triangle: if shoulders/bust are more than
~10% wider than hips, treat as top hourglass with inverted triangle modulations on top.
● Hourglass vs. bottom hourglass leaning pear: if hips are more than ~10% wider than
shoulders/bust, treat as bottom hourglass with pear modulations on bottom.
● Hourglass vs. rectangle with curves: the diagnostic is waist taper. Rectangle has minimal
waist taper even at full curve volume; hourglass has visible waist taper. Q4 fit issues
distinguish: rectangle complains of no shape; hourglass complains of pants gapping at
back.
● Spoon hourglass: shelf hips with hourglass proportions otherwise. Hourglass rules apply;
add pear modulations for hip-emphasis avoidance if user signals it.
Things behavior data should resolve:
● Bodycon comfort level (lean hourglass + bodycon doesn't always mean she wants to
wear it; learn from behavior)
● Bust featuring preference and depth tolerance (default moderate for full bust + medium
exposure; learn whether she goes deeper or stays more covered)
● Stretch fabric preference (some hourglass users prefer stable wovens with tailoring;
others rely on stretch)
● Curvy-fit brand preferences (which brands cut for hourglass; learn which user gravitates
toward)
● Midsection comfort (some hourglass users tuck religiously; others avoid waistbands
cutting into skin)
● Specific length tolerance (midi vs. knee vs. mini at various heights)

● Hip emphasis preference (low-rise opt-in; default neutral but learn whether user
gravitates toward hip-emphasis cuts)
● Curve amplification preference (sculpt opt-in; default trace/anchor but learn whether user
buys peplum, corseted, fit-and-flare with dramatic shaping)
● Closed-neckline tolerance for full-bust users (default permissive when tucked; learn
whether user actually wears crew/mock/turtleneck or avoids them regardless of styling)
Body Type: Pear
Body Type: Pear
1. Identification
How users self-identify in Q2: "My hips are wider than my shoulders and bust."
Confirming signals from Q3 (distribution):
Snug zones — diagnostic:
● Hips and thighs together (the universal pear signal — fuller lower body)
● Seat (pear typically has projected seat)
● Hips/thighs and seat all snug (strong pear signal)
● Thighs only (athletic pear variation)
Snug zones — noisy / not diagnostic:
● Chest only (if reported alone without hip signals, suggests inverted triangle or hourglass
rather than pear)
● Arms (full-bottom pear sometimes carries weight in upper arms, but not diagnostic on its
own)
● Waist/stomach (rarely snug for pear; if reported, suggests pear + midsection-prone
variation)
Loose zones — diagnostic:
● Chest/bust (pear typically has smaller bust relative to hips)

● Shoulders (narrower shoulders compared to hips)
● Arms (often lean upper body)
● Waist (defined waist between small upper body and full lower body)
Loose zones — noisy:
● Hips and thighs loose (would contradict pear classification)
Confirming signals from Q4 (fit issues):
Fit issues — diagnostic:
● Pants gap at the back waistband (universal complaint — small waist + full hip/seat)
● Pants too tight at the thigh when fitted at the waist
● Buying bottoms one size up for thighs → waist gaps
● Dresses fit hips but loose at bust
● Buying dresses to fit hips → bodice is loose and ill-fitting
● Tops fit fine but bottoms are difficult
● "Bottoms are always the hard part" — strong pear signal
Length issues — diagnostic:
● Pants feel too short → if tall
● Pants feel too long → if petite
● Mid-thigh shorts ride up too much (fuller thighs)
● Mini skirts feel proportionally wrong (hit at widest hip point on petite pear)
● "Everything usually sits where it should" — possible for mid-height pear who has
adapted buying patterns
Conflicting or ambiguous signals to watch for:
● Snug at chest/bust + snug at hips: possibly bottom hourglass leaning pear, rather than
true pear. The chest signal matters — if bust is also full, hourglass rules apply more.
● Loose at hips and thighs: contradicts pear. Check whether geometry was misreported or
whether user is rectangle with sloped shoulders.
● Snug at waist + snug at hips: possibly pear + midsection-prone variation
(post-pregnancy, post-menopause, natural soft midsection). Still pear if hips remain
widest; rules adjust to account for softer waist definition.
● Loose at bust + snug at hips + sloped shoulders: classic pear, often petite or with smaller
frame upper body. Pear rules apply strongly.
Distinctions from adjacent body types:
● Pear vs. hourglass: pear has hips noticeably wider than bust (more than 5%). Hourglass
has bust and hips balanced. If bust is small relative to hips, it's pear.

● Pear vs. bottom hourglass: bottom hourglass has hips slightly larger than bust but still
relatively balanced. Pear has a more dramatic difference between upper and lower body.
Threshold: if hips are more than ~10% wider than bust, treat as pear.
● Pear vs. rectangle: rectangle has minimal waist taper and balanced shoulders/hips. Pear
has both defined waist and wider hips. The hip-shoulder ratio is the key differentiator.
● Pear vs. apple: apple has widest point at midsection. Pear has widest point at
hips/thighs. Apples often have narrower hips and legs; pears have narrow waist and full
legs.
● Pear vs. spoon: spoon is a pear variation with shelf-hips (sharp horizontal projection at
hip). Same rules apply with stronger emphasis on managing the hip-shelf line.
2. Geometric Truth
Core proportions:
● Hips and thighs are the widest part of the body
● Shoulders are narrower than hips (sometimes by 5-15%+)
● Bust is typically smaller (B cup or smaller is common, though pears can have larger
busts; bust is just smaller than hips)
● Waist is defined and noticeably narrower than hips
● Seat has projection and shape from the side
● Thighs are full, often touching at the top
● Calves and ankles vary — can be lean or proportionally fuller
Where volume sits:
● Hips, thighs, seat — the primary volume zones
● Sometimes upper thighs and outer hips specifically
● Bust, shoulders, upper arms typically lean
● Waist is the narrowest zone
Length tendencies:
● Variable across pear users
● Pear can have proportionally shorter legs (especially when hip volume reads as visually
shortening)
● Tall pear (170+) has more visual length to balance the hip volume; petite pear (under
160) has the hardest proportion challenge
Common geometric variations within the type:

● Lean pear: smaller overall frame, hips still wider than bust but in absolute terms not
large. More styling tolerance — most cuts work because total body volume is small.
Athletic legs often present.
● Soft/fleshy pear: fuller hips, thighs, seat with naturally soft musculature. Stretch fabrics
matter critically. Tailoring becomes essential because off-the-rack often pulls in wrong
places. Heavier weight tends to settle further into the lower body.
● Athletic pear: muscular thighs and seat (sprinter, cyclist, dancer body type). Bottom
volume is muscle, not soft tissue. Same proportions as fleshy pear but firmer feel.
Stretch matters but for different reasons (muscle volume needs stretch room without
bunching).
● Spoon pear: sharp horizontal shelf at the hip line — hips project outward and then drop
relatively straight. Different from soft pear where hip curves more gradually. Bottoms can
fit at the shelf and sag below, or fit below and pull at the shelf.
● Petite pear: same proportions at smaller scale. Hip volume reads as visually large
relative to overall body length. Hardest variation to balance because all the standard
pear styling moves (long lines, structured upper body) are at a smaller scale.
● Pear + larger bust: less common but real. Hips still widest, but bust also fuller. Pear rules
apply but with more bust-management considerations (necklines, fit at top).
● Pear + midsection-prone: post-pregnancy or natural soft midsection. Waist still defined
relative to hips but less sharply. Styling adjusts for softer waist definition.
3. Visual Assets (default, before strategy modulation)
Natural assets (read well without intervention):
● Defined waist: the central asset alongside the natural lower-body shape. Smaller than
both bust and hips, creating a clear taper that styling can emphasize.
● Upper body proportions: shoulders, arms, and bust often read as delicate or
proportional, making the upper body easy to dress without bulk.
● Décolletage and collarbones: often a feature for smaller-bust pears since the upper
chest area is prominent without bust dominance.
● Lean upper back: often overlooked but a real pear asset — the upper back is typically
narrow and clean, making low-back items and back-detail items uniquely flattering.
● Curves (when celebrated): the lower-body shape is itself an asset for users who want to
feature it. Beyoncé, J.Lo, Kim Kardashian represent the pear who treats hips as the focal
point rather than something to balance away from.
● Smaller bust: often makes high necklines, button-downs, and structured tops fit without
pulling — easier upper-body fit than hourglass or full-bust users.
Features that become assets when used intentionally:

● Upper-body skin reveal as signature play: the upper body is pear's leanest, most
proportional zone. Unlike other body types that have constraints on upper-body reveal
(hourglass cleavage management, inverted triangle shoulder amplification, apple
midsection sensitivity), pear has no inherent constraint beyond user preference.
Off-shoulder, low-back, chest cutouts, halter, crop tops, and similar items feature lean
assets (décolletage, collarbones, shoulders, upper back, defined waist via midriff)
without creating proportion problems. This is one of pear's distinctive strengths — a
featuring move uniquely strong for this body type.
● Hip emphasis (opt-in): low-rise bottoms, hip-detail cuts, prints and color at the hip line,
bodycon dresses that follow hip curves. For users who want to feature the lower body
rather than balance away from it.
● Upper body amplification (balance mode): structured shoulders, statement sleeves,
embellished necklines, bright/printed tops with simple bottoms. Creates visual balance
by adding upper-body weight.
● Lengthening the silhouette: vertical lines, monochromatic outfits with subtle color shifts,
pointed shoes, elongated heels.
Override conditions:
● Lower body to be minimized (default for most pears who come to styling for help):
surface upper-body emphasis, dark bottoms, A-line skirts that skim hips, structured
shoulders. The classic "balance" approach.
● Lower body to be featured (opt-in via signals): surface hip-emphasis cuts, low-rise,
bodycon, bright bottoms, prints below the waist. The "celebrate" approach. Detected
through: behavior on hip-emphasis items, exposure preferences for the lower body,
explicit signaling on featuring-question about wanting to feature lower body.
● Upper body to be amplified: default for users in balance mode; opt-in for celebrate-mode
users.
● Upper body to be kept minimal: opt-in for users who explicitly want minimal volume on
top, often paired with celebrate-mode lower body.
● Featuring zones from new question (cross-body framework): the featuring-intent
question identifies which zones the user wants emphasized. For pear, named
upper-body zones unlock the reveal/sophisticated/cover modes for that zone. Named
lower-body zones shift toward celebrate mode.
4. Visual Risks (default, before strategy modulation)
Silhouettes that fight this body type (default balance approach):
● Skinny jeans and tight tapered pants: cling to the hip, thigh, and calf, emphasizing the
volume difference between hip and upper body. Not universally bad — celebrate-mode

users may want this — but in balance mode, they make the hip-thigh contrast more
visible.
● Tight pencil skirts: cling to hips and thighs, emphasizing hip width.
● Bodycon dresses: follow every curve, including the dramatic ones. Filtered in balance
mode; surfaced in celebrate mode.
● Hip-length jackets and tops: end at the widest hip point, creating a horizontal break that
emphasizes hip width.
● Tops that flare at the hip without compensation: peplums, dropped yokes, gathered
hems at the hip — add volume to the already-wide hip area in balance mode.
● Low-rise bottoms (in balance mode): sit at the widest hip line, widening visual hips. In
celebrate mode, this is the point and they become a hero.
● Heavy/stiff fabrics in bottoms: don't drape over the curve, add visual bulk to lower body.
● Bright colors or bold prints on bottoms (in balance mode): draw attention down.
Celebrate mode reverses this.
● Cargo pants, pleated pants with hip detail, paneled hip seams: add visual interest at the
hip.
● Drop-waist dresses: drops the waist line below natural, eliminating the asset and
emphasizing the column from shoulder to mid-thigh.
● Empire-waist dresses (sometimes): though some sources recommend empire to skim
over the hip, others note it eliminates the waist asset. Treat as situational.
● Layers that add bulk at the hip: loose tank tops under tops that flare at the hip; heavy
cardigans ending at hip line.
● Shapeless tunic tops: hang straight from shoulder to mid-thigh, hide the waist, and end
at hip-widest.
Construction details that fail (balance mode):
● Patch pockets at the hip or back pockets too close together: draw eye to hip
● Horizontal seams at the hip line: widen visually
● Embellishments at the hip, thigh, or seat: drawing attention to volume
● Pleats at the front waist: add bulk at the hip transition
● Big buttons or details down the front of skirts: draw eye to lower body
● Heavy embroidery or print placement at the hip
Fabric behaviors that fail:
● Stiff bottoms that don't drape over the curve: heavy denim, stiff canvas, thick stiff wool.
The fabric stands away from the hip and adds visual volume.
● Thin clingy bottoms on fleshy pear: show every texture and can pull badly across the
curve.
● Heavy fabric overall on lower body: compounds the volume.
Necklines that fail (default balance mode):

● Narrow high necklines (slim turtleneck, mock neck without volume): minimize the upper
body when balance mode wants the opposite.
● Plunging deep V without structure: draws eye down and into the body without
compensating volume at shoulders.
In celebrate mode these failures often reverse — narrow high necklines become tools to keep
top simple while lower body features.
Length traps:
● For petite pear: midi skirts often hit at calf-widest, doubling the hip-and-calf visual weight;
mini skirts can read disproportionate; bermuda shorts often hit at thigh-widest.
Knee-length or just-above-knee usually works best.
● For tall pear: standard pants often too short, especially when sized up for thighs (longer
legs need longer pants).
● For mid-height: usually fewer length traps but standard mid-thigh shorts can ride up.
Override conditions:
● Skinny jeans, bodycon, hip-emphasis items: hero items in celebrate mode, not failures.
● Low-rise bottoms: same — hero in celebrate mode.
● Bright bottoms and prints: heroes in celebrate mode.
● Cropped tops: work for both modes — they end above the hip, exposing the waist.
Particularly good for both balance (creates waist focus) and celebrate (frames hips by
showing the meeting point) approaches.
5. Strategy Modulation
Trace
What trace means for this body type: Trace works for pear when the fit is correct — meaning
curvy-fit cuts that accommodate the waist-to-hip ratio. Standard cuts often fail because they're
designed for proportional bodies. Trace honors the body's actual shape and lets the user decide
through other styling whether to draw eye up (balance) or down (celebrate). Pear has a clearer
body shape than rectangle or inverted triangle (defined waist + clear curves), so trace produces
a coherent silhouette when fit is right.
What works under trace:
● Fitted tops in soft fabric or stretch (work for the lean upper body)
● Wrap tops and surplice tops
● Fitted button-downs in stretch fabric
● Tucked-in fitted knit tops

● Curvy-fit jeans (the universal pear need)
● High-rise straight or slim jeans that fit the waist and skim the hip
● Tapered trousers with hip ease and waist fit
● Sheath dresses cut for curvy fit (rare but real)
● A-line skirts (technically trace + anchor — trace at the waist, A-line below as light sculpt)
What fails under trace:
● Standard-cut tops with no shaping (pull at hip when long enough to cover hips)
● Stiff fabric bottoms that don't follow hip curve
● Tight clingy bottoms in cheap stretch (cling to thighs and create texture pulls)
● Skinny jeans in stiff denim (cling wrong, no give for thighs)
Special considerations:
● Curvy-fit availability is the single biggest factor for trace success on pear. Brands that cut
for pear-specific proportions (Madewell Curvy, Levi's Wedgie, Universal Standard, etc.)
make trace possible; standard cuts often don't.
● For lean pear: most reasonable trace items work.
● For soft/fleshy pear: stretch is critical; tailoring or curvy-fit cuts are critical.
● Trace serves both balance and celebrate modes equally — the items follow the body
either way; the rest of the outfit determines direction.
Anchor
What anchor means for this body type: Universally useful for pear. The waist is the asset,
and emphasizing it via tucking, belting, wrap, or fitted bodice all work. Anchor is particularly
powerful for pear because the waist is the meeting point between the smaller upper body and
the fuller lower body — emphasizing it creates the most flattering proportion read regardless of
whether the user is in balance or celebrate mode.
Hard vs. soft anchor: Both work because the waist exists. Same logic as hourglass — almost
any anchor mechanism succeeds because there's real waist underneath. Particularly important:
anchor allows pear users who prefer relaxed-fit clothing to still preserve waist visibility. A belt
over a loose top + flowy skirt creates the proportion clarity that pure relaxed fit would lose.
Specific anchor moves that work:
● Wrap dresses and wrap tops
● Belted dresses (belt at natural waist)
● Belted shirt dresses
● Belted blazers and coats
● Tucked-in tops (full tuck, half tuck — preserves waist visibility)
● High-rise bottoms (creates implicit anchor; pear's most reliable bottom pattern)
● Cropped tops ending at the natural waist (exposes the waist meeting point)

● Wrap tops in fitted or relaxed cuts
● Dresses with built-in waist seams matching natural waist
● Statement belts over relaxed tops
Anchor moves that fail:
● Drop-waist (drops the visual waist below natural, eliminates the asset)
● Empire-waist (raises the visual waist above the actual waist, creates column from bust)
● Cinches at wrong position (paperbag above natural waist)
● Wide belts that cover too much torso (especially petite)
● Hip belts (defeats waist emphasis)
Special note on anchor + balance vs. anchor + celebrate: Anchor is mode-neutral —
emphasizing the waist serves both directions. In balance mode, anchor combined with
upper-body emphasis (statement sleeve, bright top, reveal at chest/back/shoulders) creates the
classic "draw eye up + define waist + skim hips" pattern. In celebrate mode, anchor combined
with hip-emphasis bottoms creates the "tiny waist + dramatic hip" pattern that celebrates the
curves rather than balancing them.
Sculpt
What sculpt means for this body type: Sculpt for pear operates differently depending on
mode:
Balance mode: sculpt on the upper body — structured shoulders, peplum tops,
statement-shoulder blazers, padded shoulders — creates artificial upper-body volume that
balances the lower body. This is the classic styling-literature approach.
Celebrate mode: sculpt that amplifies the lower body — corseted bodice + dramatic full skirt,
fit-and-flare that exaggerates the hip flare, mermaid skirts that fit tightly through the hips and
dramatically flare. Less common in mainstream literature but a real opt-in.
What good upper-body sculpt looks like for pear (balance mode):
● Tailored blazers with subtle shoulder structure (not heavy pads — modern soft shoulder
structure)
● Peplum tops with structured shoulders (volume both at shoulder and hip means hip
emphasis is countered)
● Structured-shoulder jackets cropped at the waist
● Tops with built-in bust or chest detail (princess seams, ruching at the bust)
● Boat-neck tops in structured fabric that widens the shoulder line
What good lower-body sculpt looks like for pear (celebrate mode):
● Mermaid skirts and dresses (fitted through hip, dramatic flare at knee)
● Fit-and-flare dresses with dramatic skirt volume that amplifies hip flare

● Corseted bodice + full skirt where the corset emphasizes the waist and the skirt amplifies
hip projection
● Pencil skirts that follow the hip closely (sculpt-as-following rather than sculpt-as-creating)
What bad sculpt looks like:
● Sculpt that adds volume at the hip line in balance mode (peplum, dropped yoke at the
hip on a balance-mode user)
● Sculpt with stiff fabric that fights the body's give (especially soft pear)
● Sculpt that imposes a waist different from the user's natural waist position
● Heavy structured shoulder pads in modern contexts (read as costume-y)
Special considerations:
● Sculpt is mode-dependent more than it is for any other body type. The same item
(peplum) is hero or filter depending on mode.
● Modern shoulder sculpt is soft — set-in sleeves with shoulder shaping, subtle pads,
structured but not aggressive. Heavy 80s shoulder pads rarely work.
● Athletic pear with muscular legs may want lower-body sculpt that follows the muscle
definition (pencil skirts, fitted leather pants, bodycon) — the body has its own structure
already.
Edge
What edge means for this body type: Edge for pear splits more cleanly than for any other
body type, because the body's natural focal point can be redirected up or down based on user
preference. Pear has a uniquely strong upper-body featuring vocabulary because the upper
body is the lean, proportional zone — skin reveal, cutouts, and play-with-shape items work there
with fewer constraints than any other body type.
Featuring modes (balance — upper body):
Upper-body skin reveal (pear signature):
● Shoulder and collarbone reveal: off-shoulder/Bardot tops and dresses (universally
flattering for pear — broadens shoulder line AND features lean shoulders); one-shoulder
asymmetric (diagonal break + skin reveal); cold-shoulder cutouts (less aggressive);
halter (narrow at neck, features upper back and shoulder)
● Chest reveal: keyhole cutouts; drop-front necklines; geometric chest cutouts; deeply
open button-ups; square neck (wide and shallow, exposes upper chest); sweetheart and
strapless for smaller-bust pears
● Back reveal: low back dresses and tops, open back, scoop back, low V back, lace-up
back details, halter neck with bare back. The back is a lean zone for pear that most other
body types can't feature as readily.

● Waist reveal: crop tops exposing the midriff. Pear midriff is often lean and defined;
exposing it features the central waist asset directly.
Upper-body emphasis without skin reveal:
● Statement sleeves (puff, bishop, bell, flutter)
● Bold print or color on top with simple bottoms
● Embellished bodice (sequins, beading, lace placement)
● Decorative collars and neckline detail
● Boat neck or wide neckline without bare shoulder
● Statement earrings and jewelry pulling eye up
● Color-blocked top with darker bottom
Décolletage and bust features:
● Open necklines (V, scoop, sweetheart) at depth calibrated to comfort
● Fitted bodice that shows shape without revealing skin
● Wrap front showing décolletage at moderate depth
Waist features:
● Statement belts
● Color-blocked waist
● Fitted-waist construction with contrasting fabric above and below
Featuring modes (celebrate — lower body):
● Hip features: hip-emphasis cuts, low-rise bottoms, hip-detail items, contoured
waistbands that follow the hip curve
● Seat features: fitted pants and skirts that follow the seat projection, bodycon dresses,
leggings as outerwear
● Thigh features: slits, side cutouts on dresses, leggings, fitted pants in dramatic fabric
(leather, vinyl, etc.)
● Leg features: fitted bootcut and flared pants (shapes the leg), cropped pants with ankle
reveal, slim trousers, monochromatic leg-extending palettes with pointed shoes
● Bottom prints and color: bright bottoms, printed skirts, embellished pants — draws eye to
the lower body intentionally
Balance through volume play:
● Volume on top + fitted bottom (less common for pear; works when top has shoulder
structure and bottom is high-rise straight)
● Fitted top + volume on bottom (volume that skims hips — A-line, wide-leg trousers —
preserves waist while balancing the silhouette)
● Monochromatic for elongation

Edge moves that fail:
● In balance mode: anything that draws eye to the lower body (bright bottoms, prints
below, hip-detail cuts) defeats the goal
● In celebrate mode: anything that minimizes the hip or shifts focus heavily upward defeats
the goal
● In either mode: edge moves that hide the waist defeat the central asset
Three-mode framework for upper-body featuring:
Most pear users will engage with upper-body featuring in one of three modes (per zone — bust,
shoulders, back, arms, midriff). The mode determines which featuring items get prioritized:
● Reveal mode: sustained skin reveal acceptable. Surface bare-shoulder off-shoulder,
deep V, fully open back, crop tops, halter with bare shoulders.
● Sophisticated mode: featuring through fit, line, and framing without sustained exposure.
Surface boat neck (broadens without bare shoulder), keyhole back, asymmetric
necklines that frame, fitted bodice with moderate neckline, sheer panel insets.
● Coverage mode: zone is featured through emphasis (fit, structure, color, detail) but skin
stays covered. Surface high necklines with detail, structured shoulders with long sleeves,
fitted-waist tops with closed neckline, color/print emphasis without skin reveal.
Detection happens through the featuring question, exposure preferences, and behavior.
Sophisticated mode is the most common; default to it when signals are ambiguous.
6. Combinational Rules
The balance vs. celebrate mode signal:
This is the central modulator for pear. Detection signals:
● Balance mode default: user reports hips/thighs/seat as snug as fit issues, signals desire
to minimize lower body, marks hips/seat as preferred-covered in exposure, doesn't name
hips/seat/legs as featuring zones, or behavior data shows preference for
upper-body-emphasis items.
● Celebrate mode opt-in: user names hips, seat, or legs as featuring zones; opts for high
exposure on lower body; behavior shows preference for hip-emphasis cuts (low-rise,
bodycon, fitted pants in statement fabrics); explicitly references wanting to feature
curves.
● Mode unclear: default to balance mode (more conservative), but watch behavior data to
learn whether the user actually gravitates toward celebrate-mode items.

A follow-up question would resolve this cleanly when needed: "Would you prefer outfits that
balance attention away from your hips, or outfits that celebrate and feature your lower body?"
Two visual examples. Worth adding to the questionnaire for pear users specifically if the
featuring-zone question doesn't resolve it.
Body + strategy combinations:
● Pear + trace: surface curvy-fit trace items. Works for both modes — fit follows the body
either way.
● Pear + anchor: surface aggressively. Universal hero strategy regardless of mode. Critical
for relaxed-fit pear users.
● Pear + sculpt + balance mode: surface upper-body sculpt (structured shoulders, peplum
tops). Filter peplum if it adds volume at hip-widest.
● Pear + sculpt + celebrate mode: surface lower-body sculpt (mermaid, fit-and-flare with
dramatic skirt, corseted with full skirt). Less commonly surfaced item set; gated by clear
celebrate signal.
● Pear + edge + balance mode: surface upper-body features (statement neckline, sleeves,
top prints/color, upper-body skin reveal at appropriate mode), waist features,
décolletage. Filter hip-emphasis edge.
● Pear + edge + celebrate mode: surface hip features, seat features, leg features, bottom
prints/color. Filter upper-body-only features unless balanced with hip emphasis.
● Pear + trace only: curvy-fit catalog; both modes work; user's other signals determine
direction.
● Pear + multiple strategies: widen catalog; sophisticated pear dressers can use all
strategies based on outfit context.
Body + fit combinations:
● Pear + close everywhere: requires trace + curvy-fit + waist-emphasis. Works for
celebrate mode; in balance mode usually paired with structured upper body to add
volume.
● Pear + close top + relaxed bottom: classic balance-mode pattern. Fitted top + A-line
skirt, wide-leg trousers, or fluid drape bottom skims hips.
● Pear + relaxed top + close bottom: less common; works when relaxed top is
belted/tucked and close bottom is celebrate-mode chosen.
● Pear + relaxed everywhere: requires anchor compensation. Belted relaxed dress,
drawstring-waist loose top + flowy skirt with defined waist meeting point. Pure shapeless
without waist visibility = universal pear failure.
Body + fabric combinations:
● Lean pear + fabric flexibility: most fabrics work.
● Soft/fleshy pear + stretch importance: stretch in bottoms weighted up significantly.
Curvy-fit cuts in non-stretch fabric still work if tailored.

● Athletic pear + stretch and structure: stretch needed for muscle accommodation;
structure needed because soft drape can look unflattering on muscular legs.
● Pear + cling-averse: filter thin clingy bottoms; surface structured stretch (ponte, sturdy
stretch denim, ribbed knits).
● Pear + stiff-fabric-averse: filter heavy stiff bottoms; surface drapey fabrics that follow the
curve.
Body + bust combinations:
● Pear + smaller bust (most common): surface a wide range of necklines including high
necks, boat necks, off-shoulder, square. The smaller bust means many necklines work
without bust-management concerns.
● Pear + larger bust: less common combination. Apply hourglass-bust rules to neckline
selection (V, scoop, sweetheart at appropriate depth). Pear lower-body rules still apply.
Body + exposure combinations:
● Pear + legs uncovered: shorter hems available for users with proportional or featured
legs; in celebrate mode this is the point. In balance mode, knee-length or longer typically
preferred to elongate.
● Pear + chest covered: less of a constraint than for hourglass because pear typically has
smaller bust. Higher necklines work for most pears. Cutouts and reveal items shift to
back, shoulder, and waist categories instead.
● Pear + back uncovered + edge: signature unlock. Open-back dresses and tops become
heroes, not just evening options. This is one of pear's most distinctive featuring
categories.
● Pear + shoulders uncovered + edge: similar — off-shoulder, Bardot, and one-shoulder
become heroes across registers, not just available items.
● Pear + lower body covered: signals balance mode. Surface upper-body emphasis
aggressively; surface longer hems; filter hip-emphasis items.
● Pear + lower body uncovered + edge: signals celebrate mode. Surface hip-emphasis,
mini hems (with proportion check), slit dresses, leggings as outerwear if user style
supports.
Body + featuring-zone combinations:
The featuring-zone question identifies which body parts the user wants emphasized. For pear:
● Pear + named upper-body zones (bust, shoulders, back, arms, décolletage, waist):
surface featuring items for those zones at the user's detected mode
(reveal/sophisticated/cover). Balance mode is typically reinforced.
● Pear + named lower-body zones (hips, seat, legs): shifts toward celebrate mode.
Surface featuring items for those zones at the user's detected mode.
● Pear + named upper AND lower zones: sophisticated multi-strategy user. Surface
featuring items for both. Likely confident dresser comfortable across modes.

● Pear + no zones named: falls back to body-type defaults (balance mode). Behavior data
refines over time.
Body + variation modulators:
● Lean pear: most cuts work; broader styling tolerance.
● Soft/fleshy pear: stretch and curvy-fit cuts essential; structured stretch fabrics weighted
up; pure stiff wovens deprioritized in bottoms.
● Athletic pear: stretch for muscle accommodation; structure to flatter muscular legs;
sometimes celebrates the lower body more readily because the muscle is itself an asset.
● Spoon pear: careful with mid-rise bottoms (can sit on shelf and look wrong); high-rise sits
above the shelf, low-rise sits below; mid-rise is the riskiest.
● Petite pear: short jackets ending at waist; knee or just-above-knee skirts; downweight
maxi unless proportions work; downweight midi unless slit or with leg break.
● Pear + midsection-prone: still hourglass-style waist emphasis but with more structural
support; surface fit-and-flare with built-in shaping; ponte and structured stretch.

● Drop-waist dresses

● Stiff fabrics in bottoms that don't drape over the curve

● Heavy front-pleat trousers with hip detail

Balance mode disqualifies:
● Bodycon dresses
● Tight pencil skirts (unless very confident user)

● Voluminous upper-body items that hide the bust/shoulder area
● Heavy structured shoulder pads that compete with hip volume
● Tops with very loose hem that hides waist
● Items that draw all attention to upper body alone

● Mini skirts disqualified for: petite pear + balance mode (proportionally too short)

● Pencil skirts disqualified for: pear who wants to minimize hips
● Skinny jeans disqualified for: pear + balance mode + no upper-body volume

● Empire-waist disqualified for: pear who wants waist-emphasis
● Cropped jackets above waist disqualified for: pear + balance mode wanting longer lines

● Off-shoulder, low-back, halter, cutouts disqualified for: pear + coverage mode on the

● Bare-shoulder reveal disqualified for: pear + sophisticated or coverage mode on

● Crop tops disqualified for: pear + cover mode on midriff (surface fitted-waist alternatives

● Mid-rise bootcut jeans on spoon pear: the mid-rise sits on the hip shelf, creating

● Cropped pants on petite pear: cropped pants in standard length often hit at calf-widest,
shortening and adding visual hip weight. Surface full-length or above-the-knee shorts
instead.
● Knee-length pencil skirts marketed as universal: fine on lean pear; on fleshy pear, the
tight skirt at knee-widest can pull. Stretch and slit matter.
9. The Uniform
Daytime / casual uniform (mid-height, mid-sophistication, balance mode):
A pair of high-rise curvy-fit straight or bootcut jeans in mid or dark wash, sitting at the natural
waist without back gap. The bootcut adds subtle volume at the ankle that tapers attention
through the leg line; the straight cut works with the jeans cut for hip ease. The waist sits at the
actual waist, not below.
On top, a fitted top in soft fabric (ribbed knit, fine cotton, ponte) tucked into the jeans. The top
has interest — could be a wrap front, a fitted button-down with rolled sleeves, a fitted V or scoop

neck, a top with a soft statement sleeve, or an upper-body reveal piece like a cold-shoulder top
or keyhole-back blouse. The interest is at the top half of the outfit.
Optionally, a cropped blazer or open denim jacket ending at the natural waist, worn open. The
jacket has subtle shoulder structure (set-in sleeve with light shoulder shape, not boxy).
Footwear: pointed ankle boots, pointed flats, or low pointed heels — anything that elongates the
leg line.
Daytime / casual uniform (celebrate mode):
High-rise or mid-rise fitted jeans (skinny, slim, or bodycon-fit) that follow the hip and thigh curve.
A simple fitted tucked top — crew neck, V-neck, or scoop — in soft fabric. The top is simple; the
bottom is the focal point. Optional belt at the waist to emphasize the waist-to-hip ratio.
Footwear: anything that follows the silhouette — ankle boots, fitted boots, heels, or sneakers
depending on register.
Variations by strategy:
● Trace-primary pear (either mode): fitted curvy-fit pieces. Trace + balance: A-line skirt
with fitted top. Trace + celebrate: straight jeans with fitted top following the curve.
● Anchor-primary pear (either mode): wrap dresses, belted everything, tucked-in tops. The
waist is the focal point regardless of mode direction.
● Sculpt-primary pear (balance): structured-shoulder blazer + dark trouser; peplum top
with structured shoulders + slim bottom.
● Sculpt-primary pear (celebrate): mermaid skirt + simple top; fit-and-flare with dramatic
skirt and simple bodice.
● Edge-primary pear (balance + reveal): off-shoulder top + high-rise dark jeans; halter
dress; low-back dress with A-line skirt.
● Edge-primary pear (balance + sophisticated): boat neck top + tailored trouser; keyhole
back dress; statement sleeve top + slim dark bottom.
● Edge-primary pear (celebrate): bodycon dress; bright printed skirt + simple top; low-rise
jeans + fitted top + statement belt.
Variations by register:
● Work: curvy-fit tailored trousers + fitted button-down (curvy fit); pencil skirt (when
appropriate) + tucked knit; A-line dress with belted blazer; sheath dress in curvy fit;
coverage-mode upper-body featuring (statement sleeve, structured shoulder) for offices
where reveal isn't appropriate.
● Evening: wrap dress, fit-and-flare cocktail, off-shoulder top + tailored skirt, low-back
dresses, halter gowns, mermaid dress (celebrate), bodycon (celebrate + comfortable).
● Occasion: belted shirt dresses, A-line midis, fit-and-flare, low-back dresses for events.
Off-shoulder for formal events broadens shoulders visually and reveals lean assets.

Variations by season:
● Summer: fitted tank tops or off-shoulder tops tucked into A-line skirts or high-rise shorts;
sundresses with defined waist; off-shoulder summer dresses; halter sundresses; soft
sandals or pointed flats.
● Winter: fitted knits tucked into high-rise jeans; belted wool coats; structured-shoulder
blazers over fitted base; keyhole-back sweaters for sophisticated featuring; knee-high
boots with knee-length skirts.
10. Inference Notes for the Recommender
Default rules that fire on pear identification alone:
1. Show the waist — central rule. Filter anything that hides it without compensation (belt,
tuck, fitted base layer).
2. Surface curvy-fit cuts when available. The single biggest factor for pear satisfaction is fit.
3. High-rise bottoms preferred almost universally. Mid-rise risky for spoon pear; low-rise
gated by mode.
4. Default to balance mode unless celebrate signals fire. Most pears coming to styling want
balance; celebrate is opt-in.
5. Detect mode through behavior, exposure preferences, featuring-zone question, and
explicit signal. When unclear, default conservative.
6. Upper-body skin reveal is a signature pear strength — not just available but actively
heroed in balance mode. Off-shoulder, low-back, chest cutouts, halter, crop tops surface
for pear users who haven't specifically marked upper-body coverage. Pear has fewer
constraints on upper-body reveal than any other body type — small bust typically means
cleavage isn't a primary concern, lean shoulders mean off-shoulder doesn't amplify, lean
back means open-back items feature a clean asset.
7. Anchor items surfaced universally (both modes benefit from waist emphasis).
8. Stretch fabrics weighted up for soft/athletic pear; pure stiff wovens deprioritized in
bottoms.
9. Skim-the-hip silhouettes (A-line, bootcut, wide-leg) heroed in balance mode;
follow-the-hip silhouettes (skinny, bodycon, pencil) heroed in celebrate mode.
10. Upper-body sculpt and edge surfaced in balance mode; minimized in celebrate mode.
11. Hip-emphasis items filtered in balance mode; heroed in celebrate mode.
12. Hip-length tops and jackets filtered (universal — end at widest point).
13. Drop-waist filtered universally; empire filtered unless explicit user preference.
14. Petite + pear: short jackets ending at waist; knee or just-above-knee skirts; cautious with
midi unless slit or with break.
15. Stretch + curvy-fit critical for fleshy/athletic pear; less critical for lean pear.
16. Standard-cut bottoms filtered when curvy-fit available.

Modulators that change defaults:
● Mode signal (balance vs. celebrate): primary modulator. Reverses many defaults: skinny
jeans, low-rise, bodycon, bright bottoms all shift between hero and filter based on mode.
● Featuring-zone signals: named upper-body zones reinforce balance mode and unlock
reveal/sophisticated/cover modes for those zones; named lower-body zones shift toward
celebrate mode.
● Strategy selection: trace universal; anchor universal; sculpt mode-dependent; edge
mode-dependent.
● Fit preferences: close everywhere natural for celebrate mode; close top + relaxed bottom
natural for balance mode; relaxed everywhere requires anchor compensation.
● Fabric importance: high importance + soft pear → curvy-fit and stretch critical; high
importance + lean pear → broader fabric tolerance.
● Bust signal: most pears have smaller bust; necklines mostly unconstrained by bust.
Full-bust pear is rarer but applies hourglass-bust rules to neckline.
● Variation modulator: lean (broader tolerance), soft (stretch and tailoring essential),
athletic (stretch + structure), spoon (rise sensitivity), petite (length sensitivity).
● Exposure per zone: zone-specific exposure preferences modulate the
reveal/sophisticated/cover mode detection for each featuring zone.
Sophistication and restrictiveness signals:
● Multi-strategy + flexible answers + curvy-fit availability → widen catalog
● Trace-only + multiple covered zones + petite + soft pear → tighten significantly; surface
hero items with confidence in fit
● Mode-unclear users → conservative (balance mode) defaults; learn through behavior
● Named multiple featuring zones → confident multi-strategy dresser; widen catalog
Risk-scanning patterns common to pear:
● Whether bottoms gap at the back waistband
● Whether bottoms pull at the thigh
● Whether tops end at hip-widest
● Whether the waist line is visible in the finished outfit
● Whether fabric drapes over hip curve or stands away
● Whether sleeve volume balances or competes with hip volume
● Whether color/print placement directs eye up (balance) or down (celebrate) as intended
● Whether upper-body reveal items match the user's mode for that zone
(reveal/sophisticated/cover)
Common "knows the trick" non-traditional moves:
● Sophisticated pear dressers may wear oversized items if consistently belted or tucked
● May wear bodycon as evening piece for celebrate mode
● May wear monochrome with subtle color shift to elongate without complete uniformity

● May use color blocking with dark waist panel to imply waist within a single-piece
silhouette
● May choose statement belt over relaxed top instead of tucking
● Celebrate-mode pear may use bright bottoms + simple tops as signature look (J.Lo,
Beyoncé patterns)
● May use upper-body reveal aggressively as a balance strategy — backless dresses,
off-shoulder tops, halters — features the lean upper body to draw attention up
● May combine sophisticated reveal moves (keyhole back, sheer panels, asymmetric cuts)
for an elevated featuring approach
On rule restrictiveness vs. user variation:
Pear has the strongest mode-split of any body type. The same item can be hero or filter
depending on whether the user is in balance or celebrate mode. The same upper-body reveal
item (off-shoulder, low-back, halter) can be hero or filter depending on the user's mode for that
zone (reveal/sophisticated/cover). This makes pear harder to default-tune than rectangle or
hourglass — the system needs to detect both the overall mode (balance vs. celebrate) and the
per-zone reveal modes.
The conservative default (balance mode + sophisticated reveal mode) serves most users who
come to styling for help. The celebrate-mode opt-in serves users who already love their curves
and want to feature them. The reveal-mode opt-in for upper body serves users comfortable with
skin exposure. The cover-mode for upper body serves users who want featuring without
exposure. The recommender should detect all of these quickly through early behavior signals
and adjust surfacing logic accordingly.
11. Open Questions and Edge Cases
Cases the rules don't yet handle:
● Pear + post-pregnancy: hips often remain wider but waist softens; surface fit-and-flare
with built-in shaping; surface high-rise with structural waistbands; filter clingy at
midsection. Pear rules apply with hourglass+midsection-prone modulation.
● Pear + post-menopause: similar adjustments; sometimes pear shifts toward spoon or
apple as weight redistributes; re-classify based on current geometry.
● Pear + significant weight gain in midsection: may shift toward apple temporarily if
midsection becomes widest. Re-classify.
● Pear + significant athletic training: may shift toward more muscular pear or inverted
triangle if upper body develops. Re-classify based on current proportions.
● Pear vs. spoon distinction in catalog: spoon needs specific rise guidance that pear rules
don't fully encode. Worth deeper variation modeling if spoon users are a significant
population.

Borderline body-type identifications:
● Pear vs. bottom hourglass: threshold around 10% hip-bust difference. Below → bottom
hourglass with pear modulators. Above → pear with hourglass-bust modulator if bust is
also fuller.
● Pear vs. spoon: both have wider hips. Spoon has the horizontal shelf at the hip; pear has
more gradual hip curve. Most styling rules transfer; spoon needs specific rise care.
● Pear with broader shoulders (athletic): if shoulders are still narrower than hips, it's pear
with inverted triangle elements on top — apply softening on top while emphasizing pear
rules on bottom.
Things behavior data should resolve:
● Mode preference (balance vs. celebrate) — the single most important behavioral signal
for pear
● Per-zone reveal mode (reveal vs. sophisticated vs. cover) for each upper-body featuring

● Curvy-fit brand preferences (which brands cut for the user's specific proportions)

Body Type: Apple (Oval)
Note: "Apple" and "oval" refer to the same body type. The terms are used interchangeably in
styling resources; "oval" is increasingly preferred to avoid fruit-shaming language but "apple"
remains the more commonly recognized term. This document uses both.
1. Identification
How users self-identify in Q2: "My midsection is the widest part of my body, with narrower
hips and shoulders."
Confirming signals from Q3 (distribution):
Snug zones — diagnostic:
● Waist/stomach (universal apple signal — the midsection is where volume sits)
● Waist/stomach AND chest together (very common — apple with fuller bust)
● Back (sometimes — fuller apple may carry weight in upper/middle back)
● Chest only with snug waist (top-heavy apple, may overlap with inverted triangle)
Snug zones — noisy / not diagnostic:
● Arms (apple may carry upper arm volume, but not diagnostic on its own)
● Shoulders (if snug alone, could suggest inverted triangle; with snug waist, confirms apple
variation)
Loose zones — diagnostic:
● Hips and thighs (apples typically have lean lower body — the universal complement to
wider midsection)
● Seat (apples often have flat or minimally projected seat)
● Legs (often lean throughout)
Loose zones — noisy:
● Waist/stomach loose (would contradict apple classification)
Confirming signals from Q4 (fit issues):
Fit issues — diagnostic:

● Pants gap at the seat or back of waistband (lean lower body + fuller waist creates fit
mismatch)
● Tops pull at the midsection but fit shoulders and arms
● Buying tops one size up to fit the midsection → boxy at shoulders and chest
● Dresses fit shoulders and bust but pull at the midsection
● Pants fit at the waist but are loose at the seat
● Pants fit at the seat but cut into the waist (the universal apple bottom problem)
● "Tops are always the hard part" — strong apple signal when combined with midsection
signal
Length issues — diagnostic:
● Tops feel too short → if tall (and if she's looking for tunic-length items that aren't
available)
● Pants feel too short → if tall
● Pants feel too long → if petite
Conflicting or ambiguous signals to watch for:
● Snug at hips/thighs: contradicts pure apple. If reported with snug waist, may be apple +
bottom-fuller variation, or borderline apple/rectangle/hourglass with midsection-prone
status.
● Loose at chest/bust: possible for smaller-bust apple (less common but real). Doesn't
reclassify; the midsection signal dominates.
● Snug at shoulders: if reported with snug waist, may indicate top-heavy apple (leans
inverted triangle but waist is still the widest point). Apply apple rules with inverted triangle
modulators on shoulders.
● Defined waist + snug waist: contradictory. If user reports she has a defined waist but the
waist is also snug, she may be post-pregnancy hourglass or rectangle with new
midsection volume — not lifelong apple. Re-classify based on whether the midsection is
the widest point currently, not historically.
Distinctions from adjacent body types:
● Apple vs. rectangle: rectangle has minimal taper but waist is not the widest point — body
is roughly equal width throughout. Apple has the widest point at the midsection.
Rectangle + midsection-prone variation is the borderline case.
● Apple vs. hourglass: hourglass has waist visibly smaller than bust/hips. Apple has waist
visibly larger than bust/hips. Direct opposite signals.
● Apple vs. inverted triangle: inverted triangle has widest point at shoulders (with narrower
hips and midsection). Apple has widest point at midsection (with narrower shoulders and
hips). If shoulders and midsection are both wide but midsection is wider, it's apple. If
shoulders are wider than midsection, it's inverted triangle.
● Apple vs. pear: pear has widest point at hips. Apple has widest point at midsection.
Apples typically have narrower hips and lean legs; pears have full hips and thighs.

● Apple vs. midsection-prone rectangle: rectangle has balanced shoulders/hips with no
significant taper but midsection that's softer or fuller than peak. Apple has shoulders/hips
narrower than midsection — the midsection actively projects beyond. Continuum more
than binary; treat anyone whose midsection is the visibly widest point as apple,
regardless of history.
2. Geometric Truth
Core proportions:
● Midsection (stomach, ribcage, sometimes back) is the widest part of the body
● Shoulders and hips are typically narrower than midsection and roughly aligned with each
other
● Bust is often fuller (cup size C or larger is common, though not universal)
● Waist is undefined or fuller than bust/hips — no clear taper
● Seat is typically flat or minimally projected
● Legs are typically lean and proportionally slim relative to the torso — often the strongest
natural asset
● Arms vary — sometimes lean, sometimes carry upper-arm volume
Where volume sits:
● Midsection (stomach, ribcage), bust, sometimes upper back and upper arms
● Tendency toward leanness: hips, thighs, seat, legs, ankles
● The defining proportion mismatch: full upper-mid body, lean lower body
Length tendencies:
● Variable; no consistent torso/leg ratio
● Apple users often have proportionally shorter torsos (the midsection volume compresses
visually)
● Legs often appear longer relative to the torso, which becomes a styling asset
Common geometric variations within the type:
● Slim/lean apple: lower weight overall but midsection is still the widest part. Less stomach
to manage; styling tolerance is broader. May not even self-identify as apple if the
difference between midsection and hips/shoulders is mild.
● Soft/fleshy apple: fuller throughout the upper-mid body. Stomach more prominent. Back
and ribcage may also carry weight. Stretch fabrics matter; structure matters; covering
midsection is a stronger priority.

● Top-heavy apple (leans inverted triangle): shoulders also broader, in addition to wider
midsection. The body looks top-dominant overall. Inverted triangle softening rules apply
on top while apple rules apply at midsection.
● Full-bust apple: the most common apple variation. Larger bust adds to the visual weight
of the upper body. Bust support becomes critical; deeper open necklines work to
balance.
● Lean lower body apple: the typical apple — flat seat, narrow hips, lean thighs and legs.
Standard apple rules apply most cleanly.
● Post-pregnancy/post-menopause apple: developed from previous body type (often
hourglass or rectangle) through hormonal weight gain at midsection. The proportions are
now apple but the styling history may not be. User may still mentally identify with old
body type; classify by current geometry.
● Petite apple: same proportions at smaller scale. Midsection volume reads larger relative
to overall body length. Vertical line emphasis becomes even more important.
● Tall apple: more visual length to balance midsection. Generally has more styling options
because long vertical lines come naturally.
3. Visual Assets (default, before strategy modulation)
Natural assets (read well without intervention):
● Legs: the universal apple asset. Often lean and proportionally slim relative to the torso.
The single strongest natural asset most apple users have. Almost every apple styling
recommendation builds around featuring the legs as the focal alternative to the
midsection.
● Ankles: often slim, especially relative to the torso. Cropped pants, slim heels, ankle
straps all work.
● Calves: typically lean. Knee-length and below-knee hems show this asset well.
● Décolletage and collarbones: often a natural asset because the upper chest area sits
above the midsection. Open necklines feature this zone.
● Bust (when proportional and supported): often fuller, becomes an asset with good bra
support and appropriate necklines.
● Shoulders: often proportional or narrower than midsection. Strong shoulder line can be
created or emphasized without the broadening problem inverted triangle has.
● Face and neck: with the right neckline, the face becomes the focal point, drawing eye up
from the midsection.
Features that become assets when used intentionally:
● Vertical line: monochromatic outfits, long open jackets and dusters, vertical seams, long
necklaces — all create lengthening lines that visually slim the torso. Vertical line is the
central styling tool for apple.

● High waist position (above the midsection): anchoring at the natural ribcage line (under
the bust, above the widest point of the stomach) creates an empire-style break that
visually shortens the upper torso to its smaller portion.
● Low waist position (below the midsection): anchoring at the high hip (below the widest
point of the stomach) creates a break point that bypasses the midsection volume.
● Featured legs as primary focus: knee-length and shorter skirts, fitted pants that show the
leg line, slim trousers, ankle reveal. Apples often have the strongest leg-feature potential
of any body type.
● Statement upper body (necklines, accessories, hair): draws attention to the face and
upper chest, away from the midsection. Statement earrings, long necklaces ending
above the bust, structured shoulders.
Override conditions:
● Legs not an asset: rare for apple but possible — user covers legs in exposure, refuses
short/knee lengths, signals discomfort with leg featuring. Suppress short-length and
slim-pant defaults. Surface featuring through alternative zones (décolletage, face-frame,
vertical line emphasis).
● Bust not for featuring: if user signals bust to be minimized rather than featured
(post-pregnancy comfort, professional context, personal preference). Surface
higher-but-detailed necklines, layering, vertical line emphasis without bust focus.
● Midsection to be acknowledged or featured: uncommon, but some users want to dress
with the midsection visible rather than concealed (body-positivity styling, confident
dressers, certain aesthetic preferences). Surface fitted dresses with structural support,
skim-fit construction, items that don't hide the midsection but don't fight it either.
4. Visual Risks (default, before strategy modulation)
Silhouettes that fight this body type:
● Tight tops that cling to the midsection: fitted ribbed tees, bodycon tops, thin clingy fabric
on the torso — all reveal every contour of the midsection. The #1 universal apple failure.
● Tucked tops at the natural waist: creates a horizontal line at the widest point of the body.
Full tuck almost always fails; half-tuck and French tuck with a longer top can work if
positioned correctly.
● Cropped tops at the natural waist: same problem — horizontal line at widest point.
● Belts at the natural waist: emphasizes width where there's no actual taper. Heavy belts
especially fail.
● Bodycon dresses: follow every contour of the midsection. Universal failure for most apple
users.

● Empire-waist tops and dresses with a tummy below the seam: the seam draws attention
to the midsection directly below it, where the volume sits. Empire works only when the
wearer doesn't carry significant midsection volume below the bust.
● Drop-waist dresses: drops the visual waist to the hip line, which on apple can fall right at
the lower edge of the midsection — emphasizing the widest point.
● Built-in waist seams that hit the natural waist: same as belt at natural waist; creates a
line at the widest point.
● Shift dresses cut without flare: a column from shoulder to knee that lacks the flare
needed to skim the midsection — can read as fitted-to-widest-point.
● Boxy oversized tops without structure: hide the body completely but add visual bulk;
baggy makes the apple shape read as "lumpy" rather than smooth.
● Tunic dresses without shape: same problem if they lack any vertical line or structural
break.
● Pleats at the front waist: add bulk at the midsection transition.
● Frills, ruffles, embellishment at the midsection: draw attention to the widest point.
● Horizontal stripes across the midsection: widen visually.
● Patch pockets at the midsection or above: bulk at the wrong zone.
● Big buttons or front detail at the midsection: visual clutter at the focal point you want to
avoid.
Construction details that fail:
● Heavy embellishment at bust line that draws eye down into midsection: the
embellishment ends at the midsection, drawing focus there.
● Horizontal seaming across the midsection: widens visually.
● Heavy ruffles or peplum hitting at the natural waist: same as belt at natural waist;
emphasizes width.
● Double-breasted blazer with buttons across the midsection: visual clutter at the focal
point.
● Stiff fabric across the bust and midsection that holds away from the body: creates a
"barrel" effect.
Fabric behaviors that fail:
● Thin clingy fabric across the midsection: reveals contours.
● Stiff structured fabric that doesn't drape: stands away from the body and creates bulk at
the widest point.
● Thick, bulky knits across the torso: add visual weight to the midsection.
● Heavy embellishment on the upper body that ends at the midsection: draws eye down to
the widest point.
● Synthetic fabrics that don't breathe and cling when warm: can cling badly at the
midsection during the day.
Necklines that fail (default):

● High closed necklines (turtleneck, mock neck, crew neck) on full-bust apple: create a
wall across the upper body and emphasize the connection between bust and midsection
without visual break.
● Halter that draws all attention to the bust line: without a vertical break, can emphasize
the bust-midsection connection.
● Boat necks on full-bust apple: broaden the shoulder line without breaking the chest
panel vertically.
● Round jewel necklines that sit right at the collarbone: don't create vertical break.
Length traps:
● For petite apple: midi skirts often hit at calf-widest, creating a wider visual line at the
legs; mini skirts can read disproportionate; tunic tops without compensation can hit at
hip-widest.
● For tall apple: standard tunic lengths often run too short to cover midsection; standard
pants often too short.
● For mid-height: standard knee-length usually works well.
Override conditions:
● Bodycon, tight tops, tucked tops at waist: hero items in confident-feature mode for users
who don't want to hide the midsection. Rare but real.
● Empire waist: works for slim apples or those without significant tummy below the bust.
Filter for apple with full tummy; surface for slim/lean apple.
● Wrap dresses: depends on construction. Wrap that ties at the natural waist (the apple's
widest point) fails; wrap that ties at empire position or with a draped front that skims
rather than cinches can work.
● Skinny jeans: work when paired with long tunic tops, dusters, or long open layers that
cover the midsection. Fail when paired with cropped or fitted tops that expose the
midsection-to-leg transition.
5. Strategy Modulation
Trace
What trace means for this body type: The riskiest strategy for apple. Trace honors the body's
natural shape — but the apple's natural shape is exactly the proportion problem the user
typically wants to manage. Trace tops will trace the midsection; trace bottoms will trace the lean
legs (which is actually fine — but the midsection trace creates the failure). A user picking
trace-only is signaling either (a) very slim apple where the proportion mismatch is mild, (b)
confident dresser who doesn't want to hide the midsection, or (c) doesn't realize what trace will
do for her body.

What works under trace:
● Slim trousers (legs are lean — trace works on lower body)
● Fitted bootcut and flare pants (legs are featured)
● Slim pencil skirts when worn with tunic or long top covering midsection
● Skim-fit tops in fluid fabric (not clinging, but following the body softly)
● Wrap tops where the wrap creates a vertical line and the fabric drapes rather than clings
What fails under trace:
● Tight fitted tops at the midsection
● Bodycon dresses
● Tucked-in fitted tops
● Fitted shift dresses
● Anything that closely follows the midsection contour
Special considerations:
● Trace works for apple on the lower body (legs are an asset) but fails on the upper
body/midsection. Half-trace approaches — fitted legs + skim-fit torso — are essentially
the apple uniform.
● A user picking trace-only signals limited strategy options. Recommendations should be
hero-weighted, with careful focus on items that flatter without clinging at midsection.
Anchor
What anchor means for this body type: Anchor for apple is the trickiest of all body types. The
body doesn't have a natural waist to honor or emphasize, and anchoring at the natural waist
creates the worst failure mode (line at the widest point). Apple anchor almost always means
anchoring above or below the natural waist — empire (under the bust) or low-anchor (at the
high hip below the stomach). The natural waist itself is rarely the right anchor point.
Hard vs. soft anchor:
Empire anchor (under the bust):
● Works for slim/lean apple without significant tummy below the bust
● Works for apple with smaller bust who can create a column from under-bust down
without volume mismatch
● Fails for apple with full bust AND full tummy — the empire seam emphasizes the
connection between bust and stomach
Low anchor (at the high hip):
● Tunic tops ending below the midsection at the high hip create a visual break that
bypasses the midsection

● Long open jackets and cardigans hitting at high hip work as low anchors
● Tops with seam detail or color block hitting at high hip create implied anchor below the
widest point
Natural waist anchor:
● Almost always fails for apple
● The exception: very specific structured construction (a corseted bodice that physically
compresses while creating shape) — mostly evening wear, rare in everyday wardrobe
Specific anchor moves that work:
● Empire-waist tops and dresses (gated by tummy presence)
● Tunic tops ending below the midsection
● Long open dusters or cardigans creating vertical line + low break point
● Tops with vertical seam detail that implies anchor without cinching
● Half-tucked tops where the tuck point is below the natural waist (at the high hip)
● Layered looks where the inner layer is long enough to cover midsection and outer layer
creates structure
Anchor moves that fail:
● Belts at the natural waist (almost universally)
● Wide belts at the natural waist (severe failure)
● Drawstring at natural waist
● Built-in waist seams at natural waist
● Tucked-in tops with full tuck at natural waist
● Cropped tops at natural waist
● Drop-waist hitting at the lower stomach (can emphasize the lower edge of the
midsection)
Special considerations:
● Apple anchor strategy is more about avoiding bad anchor placement than creating new
anchor. Most apple uniforms work through vertical line + skim fabric rather than through
traditional waist emphasis.
● The "anchor below the widest point" principle (used for midsection-prone rectangle)
applies here as the default.
Sculpt
What sculpt means for this body type: Sculpt for apple is potentially useful but rarely heroic.
Structured construction can impose a smoother silhouette through the midsection — but sculpt
that imposes a defined waist where there isn't one tends to fail (looks costume-y or fights the
body). The most useful sculpt for apple is structural support that compresses or smooths without
creating a fake waist.

What good sculpt looks like for this body:
● Tailored blazers with structured shoulders and skim-fit through the torso (creates
structure without cinching)
● Fit-and-flare dresses where the fit is at the bust line (empire) and the flare bypasses the
midsection
● Structured sheath dresses that smooth the midsection rather than emphasize it
● Shapewear or supportive undergarments under draped fabric (supports the silhouette
without visible sculpt)
● Princess seams running vertically through the torso (vertical lines that imply shape
without cinching)
What bad sculpt looks like:
● Sculpt with a built-in waist seam at the natural waist
● Sculpt with heavy structured shoulders that create a "barrel" silhouette
● Bodycon sculpt that follows the midsection
● Corseted bodice that ends at natural waist (cinches at the widest point)
● Empire sculpt on apple with full tummy (the structure doesn't help)
Special considerations:
● For slim apple, sculpt is rarely needed; trace + edge often does the work
● For soft apple, sculpt becomes valuable as structural support — surface tailored blazers,
structured A-line dresses, supportive shapewear-friendly silhouettes
● Sculpt for apple isn't about creating curves; it's about smoothing the existing silhouette
Edge
What edge means for this body type: The strongest and most useful strategy for apple. Edge
redirects attention away from the midsection by featuring alternative zones — legs, décolletage,
face, ankles, shoulders. Apple has unusually clear featuring potential because the natural
assets (legs especially) are pronounced relative to the midsection. The featuring move is what
most styling literature actually recommends, even when it's framed as "balance" or "hide
tummy."
Featuring modes (the standard apple approach):
Legs as primary feature (almost universal):
● Knee-length and shorter A-line skirts
● Knee-length and shorter A-line dresses
● Slim trousers that show the leg line
● Cropped pants with ankle reveal
● Slim pencil skirts with appropriate top coverage
● Heels and pointed shoes that elongate the leg

Décolletage as feature:
● Open V-necks, deep V-necks
● Scoop necks
● Sweetheart necklines (for full-bust apple with support)
● Open button-downs
● Statement necklaces ending above the bust
Face and upper body as feature:
● Statement earrings
● Bold lipstick or strong eye makeup
● Hair styled to frame the face
● Open necklines that draw eye to the face
● Hats and headwear that pull focus up
Vertical line as feature (lengthening rather than zonal):
● Monochromatic outfits
● Long open jackets and dusters
● Long necklaces creating vertical line down the torso
● Vertical seam detail
● Color blocking with darker midsection panel within a lighter overall outfit
Three-mode framework for upper-body featuring (applies to apple):
For each named upper-body zone (décolletage, shoulders, arms, bust), the user operates in:
● Reveal mode: sustained skin reveal acceptable — deep V, sleeveless, off-shoulder,
open back at appropriate registers
● Sophisticated mode: featuring through fit, line, and framing without sustained exposure
— moderate V at collarbone, three-quarter sleeves, sheer panels, keyhole details
● Coverage mode: zone featured through emphasis without skin — high necklines with
detail, long sleeves with structure, accessory focus
Most apple users operate in sophisticated or coverage mode for the upper body, paired with
reveal or sophisticated mode for legs.
Edge moves that work:
● Knee-length A-line dresses with V-neck (legs + décolletage simultaneously)
● Slim pants with long open duster and statement earrings (legs + vertical line + face
frame)
● Tunic + slim pants + heels (legs featured, midsection skimmed, ankles visible)
● Wide-leg pants + fitted blazer + statement necklace (balanced silhouette with face-frame
edge)

● Knee-length skirt + structured top with V-neck (legs + chest panel break)
Edge moves that fail:
● Edge moves that draw attention to the midsection (statement belts at waist,
embellishment at midsection, contrast color at waist)
● Statement features at bust or hip without compensation that bypasses the midsection
6. Combinational Rules
Body + strategy combinations:
● Apple + trace: surface skim-fit trace items, fitted lower body. Filter clinging-at-midsection
items. Most restrictive strategy for apple.
● Apple + anchor: surface empire styles (gated by tummy), tunic-length tops, long open
layers, vertical seam details. Filter natural-waist anchors aggressively.
● Apple + sculpt: surface structured pieces that smooth without cinching. Filter sculpt with
natural-waist seams or built-in waist construction at the natural waist.
● Apple + edge: surface aggressively. The strongest strategy for apple. Legs, décolletage,
face-frame, vertical line all become primary tools.
● Apple + trace only: highest-restriction combination. Surface hero items with careful
midsection management; surface lean-leg featuring; very few items.
● Apple + multiple strategies: widen catalog; the system can serve a sophisticated apple
dresser through edge + structured layering + occasional empire pieces.
Body + fit combinations:
● Apple + close everywhere: fights the body almost entirely. Requires close fit + featuring
through accessories and accents rather than silhouette. Lean-leg users may want close
bottoms with skim tops.
● Apple + close top + relaxed bottom: unusual for apple (opposite of typical apple uniform).
Works only when relaxed bottom is structured (wide-leg trousers) and close top is in
compressive supportive fabric.
● Apple + relaxed top + close bottom: the classic apple uniform. Tunic, fluid top, skim-fit
jacket worn open + slim pants, fitted skirt. Surface aggressively.
● Apple + relaxed everywhere: requires structure somewhere (long jacket open, statement
accessory, vertical line). Pure shapeless = universal failure.
Body + fabric combinations:
● Apple + cling-averse: filter thin clingy fabrics across the torso; surface flowing drape that
skims; surface structured fabrics with body.

● Apple + heavy-averse: applies to upper body. Filter heavy thick knits; surface fine gauge
knits, fluid drape, lightweight structure.
● Apple + fluid drape + structure preference: the ideal combination — fluid fabric that
drapes over the midsection paired with structured layering (blazer, duster, tailored
jacket).
● Apple + bust-support importance: universal for full-bust apple. Surface items with
structural bust support; filter strapless or bra-incompatible designs unless user signals
comfort with these.
Body + bust + exposure combinations:
The bust + exposure intersection matters significantly for apple:
● Apple + full bust + high exposure preference: surface deep V, plunging styles, open
necklines. The bust featured + chest panel broken vertically.
● Apple + full bust + medium exposure + chest not flagged covered: moderate-depth open
necklines surfaced (V at collarbone-to-mid-chest, scoop at clavicle). Deep V and
plunging filtered. Same logic as hourglass; resolve ambiguity through behavior or
follow-up.
● Apple + full bust + low exposure or chest covered: higher slim necklines but with vertical
break (button placket, vertical detail, layered jewelry). Avoid pure high closed crew/mock
without compensation.
● Apple + smaller bust + any exposure: most necklines accessible. The bust isn't doing the
heavy proportion work; vertical line and midsection management still matter.
Body + leg-featuring combinations:
● Apple + legs uncovered + edge: short and knee-length hems surfaced aggressively. The
legs are the primary asset.
● Apple + legs covered: significant constraint. Apple's strongest featuring zone becomes
unavailable. Surface vertical line emphasis, face-frame, décolletage instead. Long pants
with leg-line emphasis (slim, fitted bootcut) substitute.
● Apple + legs featured at sophisticated mode: surface fitted bootcut, slim trousers,
knee-length skirts with appropriate slit, fitted pants in interesting fabric, ankle reveal.
Filter mini hems and high slits unless explicit signal.
● Apple + legs featured at reveal mode: surface mini A-line, short A-line dresses,
knee-and-above skirts, leg-revealing cuts. The reveal mode for legs is more accessible
for apple than for any other body type because legs are typically the body's strongest
asset.
Body + exposure combinations (other zones):
● Apple + chest covered: surface vertical-line tops, layering, V-shapes within closed
neckline (vertical seaming, button placket detail).

● Apple + back uncovered + edge: less of a signature unlock for apple than for pear (back
is sometimes a zone apple users want to manage). Available when user signals back is
fine to feature; not default.
● Apple + arms covered: surface long sleeves; three-quarter sleeves work well as
midsection-shortening tool.
● Apple + midriff covered: universal for apple (the midriff is the managed zone). Filter crop
tops, midriff-baring items, anything that exposes the stomach area.
Body + variation modulators:
● Slim/lean apple: broader styling tolerance; empire works; mini hems easier; some trace
items accessible.
● Soft/fleshy apple: stricter midsection coverage rules; structured layering critical; stretch
fabrics important; vertical line essential.
● Top-heavy apple (inverted triangle leaning): apply inverted triangle softening on top
(avoid structured shoulders that amplify; surface V-necks and softening) AND apple rules
at midsection.
● Full-bust apple: bust support critical; deeper open necklines work; high closed necklines
filtered.
● Post-pregnancy/post-menopause apple: may still feel emotionally like previous body
type; surface items that work for current geometry; respect that midsection sensitivity is
often higher in this variation.
● Petite apple: short jackets that end at high hip (not natural waist); knee-length skirts;
vertical line emphasis critical because shorter body has less length to balance
midsection.
● Tall apple: longer tunic and duster options; more length for vertical line emphasis; midi
skirts work better than for petite apple.

Hero items (consistent across apple/oval):
● A-line dresses (knee or just above knee)
● Fit-and-flare dresses with empire bodice (gated by tummy)
● Sheath dresses in skim-fit fabric (not clinging)
● Tunic tops ending below the midsection
● Long open dusters, cardigans, and jackets
● Slim trousers and tapered pants in mid-rise
● Bootcut and flared pants in mid- or high-rise
● Wide-leg trousers in fluid fabric
● Knee-length A-line skirts
● Pencil skirts (when worn with tunic-length top covering midsection)

● Soft drape blouses in fluid fabric
● V-neck and scoop neck tops
● Tailored blazers worn open over fitted base layer
● Wrap tops where the wrap creates vertical line without cinching at natural waist
Hero necklines:
● V-neck (especially deep V) — breaks the chest panel vertically, draws eye up
● Scoop neck — open and softening
● Sweetheart (for full-bust apple with support, at appropriate exposure)
● Surplice and wrap necklines
● Open button placket (vertical break)
● Cowl neck (drapes softly without bulk at chest)
● Asymmetric necklines (vertical break + interest)
● Off-shoulder for users comfortable with shoulder reveal
Necklines to avoid (default):
● High closed crew, mock, turtleneck on full-bust apple (creates wall, emphasizes
bust-midsection connection)
● Boat neck on full-bust apple (broadens without breaking chest)
● Halter without compensation
● Round jewel neckline at collarbone (doesn't create vertical break)
● High necks with heavy embellishment
Hero sleeves:
● Three-quarter sleeves (shortens visual midsection by 3-6 inches according to styling
sources — universal apple recommendation)
● Long fluid sleeves
● Bishop sleeves with structure
● Set-in sleeves with subtle shoulder shape
● Bell sleeves (volume at wrist that doesn't drop to midsection)
Sleeves to avoid:
● Drop-shoulder dolman
● Heavy puff sleeves at the shoulder (amplifies top-heavy reading)
● Cap sleeves on top-heavy apple (emphasizes shoulder-bust line)
● Tight short sleeves cutting into the upper arm
● Very fitted sleeves on soft-arm apple
Hero pant silhouettes:
● Mid-rise straight (rise that doesn't cut into midsection)
● Mid-rise bootcut

● Mid-rise wide-leg
● High-rise with wide flat smoothing waistband (when comfortable)
● Flared jeans (balances narrow legs with wider midsection)
● Slim trousers (legs are the asset)
Pants with specific gating:
● Skinny jeans: work when paired with long tunic top or duster that covers midsection;
filtered with cropped or fitted tops
● High-rise: gated by comfort — high-rise with smoothing waistband works; high-rise that
cuts into midsection fails
Pants to avoid:
● Pants with pleats at front waist (adds midsection bulk)
● Tight skinny jeans without long top compensation
● Low-rise (sits at lower edge of midsection, emphasizes the widest area)
● Heavy waistbands that cut into the midsection
● Tapered pants without flare (emphasizes the narrow-leg-wide-torso mismatch)
Hero dress and skirt silhouettes:
● A-line dresses (knee length)

Dresses and skirts to avoid:
● Bodycon dresses

● Tiered or ruffled skirts that add bulk at the waist

● Heavily gathered or pleated at the natural waist

Hero outerwear:
● Long open dusters

Outerwear to avoid:

Hero items by strategy:

8. Disqualified Categories
Items that don't work, regardless of strategy:
● Tight bodycon dresses
● Fitted ribbed knits across the midsection
● Tucked-in fitted tops at natural waist (full tuck)
● Cropped tops at natural waist
● Wide belts at natural waist
● Heavy chunky belts at natural waist
● Drop-waist dresses (typically — hits at lower edge of midsection)
● Empire-waist dresses for full-tummy apple
● Pleated front trousers with heavy pleating at the waist
● Tiered or ruffled skirts gathered at the natural waist
● Halter tops that pull attention to bust line without vertical break
● Bandeau and strapless tops without compensating coverage and support
● Tube dresses
● Wrap dresses that tie tightly at the natural waist
● Bodycon midi dresses

Items disqualified by specific answer combinations:
● Empire-waist disqualified for: apple with significant tummy below the bust
● Skinny jeans disqualified for: apple + cropped top preference + no long-top coverage
● Mini skirts disqualified for: petite apple unless explicit confident-feature signal
● Pencil skirts disqualified for: apple + relaxed top preference (no coverage means
midsection unmanaged)
● High closed necklines disqualified for: full-bust apple + no vertical line compensation
● Belts at natural waist disqualified for: any apple unless very specific structured
construction
Items disqualified by featuring-mode signals:
● Midriff-exposing crop tops disqualified for: apple + cover mode on midriff (default — most
apple users)
● Crop tops above natural waist available for: apple + reveal or sophisticated mode on
midriff (rare — confident dresser opt-in)
● Bodycon dresses disqualified for: apple + cover mode on midsection (default)
● Bodycon available for: apple + reveal mode on midsection (very rare — explicit opt-in)
Items that look right but disappoint:
● Wrap dresses on apple: marketed as universal hero, but the wrap tie often hits at the
natural waist (apple's widest point) and emphasizes width. Surface wrap dresses with
vertical-line construction or empire-tied wraps; filter wraps that cinch at the natural waist.
● Empire-waist dresses on apple: mainstream styling recommends empire for apple
universally, but for full-tummy apple the empire seam emphasizes the midsection directly
below. Gate by tummy presence; surface for slim/lean apple only.
● Boxy tunics: hide the body but read as shapeless and "lumpy" on apple. Surface tunics
with structure (princess seams, vertical detail, slim sleeves) rather than pure boxy.
● Bodycon with shapewear: the shapewear compresses but the bodycon still reveals every
contour. Surface skim-fit alternatives that don't rely on undergarment compression to
look right.
● Belted blazers: the belt at natural waist creates the failure mode. Surface unbelted
blazers worn open or with belt tied at the back.
● "Universal" maxi dresses: maxi dresses that gather at the natural waist fail. Surface
column maxi dresses with empire bodice or vertical-line construction.
9. The Uniform
Daytime / casual uniform (mid-height, mid-sophistication, mixed strategy):

A pair of mid-rise straight or bootcut pants in dark wash, fitting at the seat without back gap and
not cutting into the midsection at the waistband. The leg shape shows the lean lower body —
straight or with subtle bootcut flare at the ankle.
On top, a tunic-length blouse or top in fluid fabric (silk, cotton voile, fine cotton, soft modal
jersey) ending at the high hip, just below the widest point of the midsection. The top has a
V-neck or scoop neck that breaks the chest panel vertically. Three-quarter sleeves keep the
proportions long without cropping the silhouette.
Over the top, an open long cardigan, duster, or tailored blazer worn unbuttoned and unbelted.
The outer layer creates a vertical line down the body and the open front shows a strip of the
inner layer creating an implied column. The outer layer ends below the high hip — knee length
for the long duster version, just below the high hip for the tailored blazer version.
Statement necklace ending above the bust line — a long pendant or chain that creates an
additional vertical line down the chest. Statement earrings drawing eye to the face.
Footwear: pointed flats, pointed ankle boots, or low pointed heels. Anything that elongates the
leg and draws attention to ankles and feet (an apple asset zone).
Variations by strategy:
● Trace-primary apple: tunic top + slim trouser (trace the legs, skim the torso). Specific
selection because trace is restrictive.
● Anchor-primary apple: tunic + slim bottom + statement vertical-line necklace; long open
duster over fitted slim base.
● Sculpt-primary apple: tailored blazer worn open over structured A-line dress; structured
sheath dress in supportive fabric.
● Edge-primary apple: knee-length A-line dress + deep V + statement earrings (legs +
décolletage + face); slim trouser + tunic + long duster + heels (legs + vertical line +
ankle).
Variations by register:
● Work: tailored slim trousers + fluid silk blouse + open tailored blazer; sheath dress in
skim-fit fabric with V-neck; knee-length A-line dress with three-quarter sleeves and open
neckline.
● Evening: knee-length A-line dress + statement jewelry; bias-cut maxi dress with empire
bodice and vertical column skirt; structured cocktail dress with V-neck and
skim-through-midsection construction.
● Occasion: A-line dresses, knee-length sheath dresses, empire-bodice maxi dresses
(gated by tummy). Apple-friendly evening wear often involves structured bodice + flowing
skirt.
Variations by season:

● Summer: fluid sleeveless or short-sleeve tops in cotton voile or silk + slim trousers;
A-line cotton sundresses with V-neck; cropped wide-leg pants + simple top + statement
accessories.
● Winter: fine-gauge knit tunics + slim trousers + long wool coats; sweater dresses in
skim-fit fabric (not clingy) with three-quarter sleeves; structured wool blazers over fluid
base + slim trousers.
10. Inference Notes for the Recommender
Default rules that fire on apple identification alone:
1. Skim the midsection — central rule. Filter anything that clings to or cinches at the natural
waist.
2. Filter all natural-waist anchors aggressively: belts at waist, drawstring at waist, tucked
tops at waist, cropped tops at waist, built-in waist seams at natural waist.
3. Surface vertical-line items aggressively: long open dusters, long cardigans,
monochromatic outfits, vertical seam construction, long pendant necklaces.
4. Legs are typically the strongest asset; surface leg-featuring items (knee-length, short
A-line, slim pants, bootcut, ankle reveal).
5. Three-quarter sleeves preferred (universal styling-source recommendation for visual
midsection-shortening).
6. V-necks, scoops, and open necklines for upper body break the chest-midsection
connection.
7. Empire-waist gated by tummy presence: surface for slim/lean apple; filter for full-tummy
apple.
8. Wrap dresses gated by construction: surface when wrap creates vertical line; filter when
wrap cinches at natural waist.
9. Tunic-length tops preferred over hip-length and waist-length tops.
10. Bootcut and flared pants weighted up to balance narrow legs against wider midsection.
11. Skinny jeans gated by compensating top length: long tunic or duster required to surface.
12. Stretch fabrics with body weighted up; thin clingy fabrics filtered for torso pieces.
13. Petite + apple: short jackets at high hip (not natural waist); knee-length skirts; vertical
line emphasis even more critical.
14. Full-bust + apple: bust support critical; open necklines required; high closed necklines
filtered.
15. Top-heavy apple: apply inverted triangle softening on top (V-necks, no structured
shoulders) AND apple midsection management.
16. Standard pant rises gated by comfort signal: high-rise with smoothing waistband works;
high-rise that cuts into midsection fails.
Modulators that change defaults:

● Strategy selection: trace highly restricted; anchor mostly about avoiding bad placement;
sculpt mostly structural support; edge primary tool.
● Fit preferences: relaxed top + close bottom is the natural apple uniform; close
everywhere fights the body; relaxed everywhere requires structural compensation.
● Fabric importance: high importance + soft drape preferred → skim-fit fluid fabrics
weighted up; high importance + structured items → tailored layering and structured
A-line surfaced.
● Bust signal modulates neckline access significantly (full-bust apple has tighter neckline
rules; smaller-bust apple has broader access).
● Tummy signal modulates empire access (universally — empire fails for full-tummy apple,
works for slim apple).
● Featuring zones (from new question): named upper-body zones modulate neckline and
feature selection; legs almost universally named — surface aggressively when named.
Sophistication and restrictiveness signals:
● Multi-strategy + flexible answers + clear edge-featuring zones → widen catalog
● Trace-only + multiple covered zones + full-bust + soft apple → tighten significantly;
surface hero items with structural support
● Strong leg-featuring signal → unlock the strongest apple styling moves (short hems, slim
pants, ankle reveal)
● No leg-featuring signal (legs covered preference) → significant constraint; surface
vertical line and face-frame edge instead
Risk-scanning patterns common to apple:
● Whether items cling to or cinch at the midsection
● Whether the construction creates a horizontal line at the natural waist
● Whether the silhouette creates vertical lines or horizontal ones
● Whether the upper body break is at the right level (under bust = empire works for slim; at
high hip = low anchor works for everyone)
● Whether the leg asset is being featured or hidden
● Whether bust support is appropriate for the construction
● Whether fabric drapes over the midsection or stands away creating bulk
Common "knows the trick" non-traditional moves:
● Sophisticated apple dressers may wear bodycon with confidence if the styling supports it
(long open duster over bodycon, statement accessories pulling focus, confident posture)
● May use color blocking strategically — darker midsection panel within lighter outfit
creates implied slim torso
● May wear monochromatic outfits in textured fabrics (lace overlay, ribbed knit) that
provide visual interest without cinching
● May use vertical-line necklaces and accessories aggressively as constant styling tools

● May wear empire styles confidently when bust is supported and the skirt drapes
vertically rather than gathered
● May feature midsection through structured shapewear under fluid drape — the structure
smooths, the drape skims, no compression visible
On rule restrictiveness vs. user variation:
Apple is the most styling-rule-restrictive body type in mainstream literature — the universal
"skim midsection, feature legs, vertical lines" framework has near-100% agreement across
sources. But apple users also have unusually clear featuring potential: legs are almost
universally an asset, vertical line works for everyone, V-necks suit nearly all apple variations.
The rules should be confident about filtering the failure modes (natural-waist cinches, clinging at
midsection, horizontal lines at waist) and confident about surfacing the hero categories (A-line,
V-necks, vertical line, leg-featuring, three-quarter sleeves). The user variations modulate
execution (slim vs. fleshy, full-bust vs. small-bust, top-heavy vs. lower-body-lean) but rarely
overturn core rules.
The hardest user to serve is the apple who wants to dress in current trends that fight her body
(cropped tops, bodycon, belted-at-waist trends). The system should surface modified versions
where possible (cropped tops over higher-waisted long bottoms, bodycon under long dusters,
"belted" looks via vertical seam detail rather than physical belts).
11. Open Questions and Edge Cases
Cases the rules don't yet handle:
● Apple + significant weight loss: user identified as apple historically but has lost weight
and proportions are shifting toward rectangle. Re-classify based on current geometry;
expect transition discomfort as the user adjusts to new options.
● Apple + recent weight gain: user was rectangle or hourglass, now has apple-shape
midsection volume. May resist apple recommendations because they don't match
self-image. System should provide gentle transition surfacing (items that work for current
geometry but resemble previous wardrobe style).
● Apple + pregnancy: not the same body type during pregnancy. Maternity wear has its
own rules; system shouldn't apply apple rules to maternity.
● Slim apple who's actually rectangle: if midsection is only mildly the widest point, may be
functionally rectangle with mild midsection-prone signal. Re-evaluate; rectangle rules
may serve better.
● Apple with very flat seat: may benefit from pear styling techniques in reverse — adding
back-pocket detail to lift the seat shape. Not a standard apple recommendation but worth
surfacing as an option.

● Apple with unusually defined waist that's still wider than bust/hips: possible to have a
curved torso but still apple-classification. Treat with hourglass-style anchor at the actual
waist, but keep apple's other rules.
Borderline body-type identifications:
● Apple vs. rectangle midsection-prone: threshold is whether the midsection is projecting
beyond the shoulders and hips, or just equal to them. If projecting, apple. If equal but
soft, rectangle with midsection modulator.
● Apple vs. inverted triangle top-heavy: if shoulders are wider than midsection, inverted
triangle. If midsection is wider than shoulders (and hips), apple. Top-heavy apple sits at
the borderline; if midsection projects more than shoulders, treat as apple.
● Apple vs. hourglass with weight gain: if waist is still smaller than bust and hips even at
higher weight, it's still hourglass with midsection-prone modulator. If waist becomes
equal to or larger than bust/hips, it's apple.
Things behavior data should resolve:
● Tummy intensity (slim apple with minor midsection volume vs. full-tummy apple) —
affects empire-access threshold
● Bust featuring preference and depth tolerance (default moderate for full-bust + medium
exposure)
● Leg-featuring intensity (most apple users feature legs heavily, but some prefer slim pants
only over knee-and-shorter skirts)
● Bodycon and tight-fit tolerance (most apple users avoid; some explicitly want to feature
midsection rather than hide)
● Crop top tolerance (similar — most filter; some embrace)
● Belt tolerance (default filter at natural waist; learn whether user buys/wears belts despite
the rule)
● Wrap dress tolerance (different wraps work differently; learn which specific constructions
she gravitates toward)
● Vertical line aggressiveness (some apple users use long dusters constantly; others stay
minimal)
● Three-quarter sleeve preference (universal styling recommendation; learn whether user
actually prefers them or feels they're frumpy)
● Tucked vs. untucked preference (some apple users half-tuck religiously; others prefer
fully untucked tunic length)
● High-rise vs. mid-rise comfort (high-rise with smoothing band works for some; mid-rise
more comfortable for others)
